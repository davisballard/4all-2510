# 4 All Snacks — Audience Mindset Tiers

**Source:** Ishan + Elliot strategy-session input deck, slide 11. Captured 2026-05-18.

The deck divides the buying universe into **four mindset tiers** by shopping context, not by demographic. This is *where to play,* sequenced. Hero SKU launches into Tier 1 (Value Stock-Up). Tier 2 (Consumption Now) is the next move. Tiers 3 and 4 are later-arc.

---

## The four-tier model

| Mindset | Where they shop | How they shop |
|---|---|---|
| **#1 — Value Stock-Up** (START) | Mass, Club, Discount (Walmart, Costco, Sam's, Dollar General) | Big basket · price-driven / lowest cost-per-unit · multi-packs · planned provisioning |
| **#2 — Consumption Now** (next?) | C-Store, Vending, Drug (7-Eleven, gas stations, CVS) | Small basket · convenience · high impulse / brand-driven behavior · premium on cost-per-unit · single-serve |
| **#3 — Captive, Regulated, Nutrients First** | K-12 schools, hospitals | Compliance to nutritional spec · bulk pack · no merchandising · contract-driven |
| **#4 — Aligned & Brought to Me** | D2C (subscription, ecommerce) | Loyalist behavior · low discovery · low impulse · subscribe & save · frustration-free shippable packaging |

---

## Tier 1 — Value Stock-Up (Launch tier)

This is the **Walmart shelf** the founder and Kinder's are already committed to. It defines:

- **Pack format:** Multi-pack (12-count or higher), not single-serve.
- **Price ceiling:** Same shelf, same price as Otter Pops / Welch's / Gushers / Kroger pops.
- **Merchandising:** Endcap during summer activation; freezer-door block; high stacking density.
- **Shopper motion:** "Planned provisioning" — the freeze pop is on a list (or remembered route) and the basket already has milk, eggs, paper towels. The kid may not be there.
- **Decision cue:** Eye-level shelf scan, sub-2-second pickup decision, often by the parent without the kid present.

Designing the SKU for Tier 1 means: **the parent has to recognize and trust the pack visually** in the same scan-window where they're judging Honest Kids and Gushers. That's the **back-of-pack (parent territory) reverse-mullet rule** doing work.

---

## Tier 2 — Consumption Now (Wave 2)

C-store, vending, drug. **Single-serve, kid-in-the-store-with-money, premium cost-per-unit, brand-driven impulse.**

This is a *very different SKU* from the Walmart 12-pack:

- Single-serve sleeve / 1-up pack
- Hand-friendly format (the kid holds it walking out of 7-Eleven)
- Higher per-unit price (impulse premium)
- Brand recognition is everything — kid has 0.5 seconds to grab one off a refrigerated bay against 50 SKUs, mostly Slurpees, Gatorade, Vitamin Water
- Front-of-pack design is the entire purchase trigger

This tier likely runs **after** Tier 1 proves out — but the brand system being designed in the workshop should be *built to extend* into this format without redesigning from scratch.

---

## Tier 3 — Captive, Regulated, Nutrients First

K-12 schools, hospitals. **Contract-driven, nutritional-spec compliant, no shelf merchandising.**

This is the **"every school lunch, every hospital meal"** part of the deck's platform headline. The formulation (vitamins, electrolytes, no artificial color *option*, lower-sugar variant) needs to be **USDA Smart Snacks compliant** if K-12 is a real route.

Note: The deck's hero SKU formulation as currently described (sugar + some artificial OK for cost + vitamins added) is **NOT USDA Smart Snacks compliant for in-school sale.** That's a Tier-3 product variant decision, parked for the campaign book's Production Brief.

---

## Tier 4 — Aligned & Brought to Me

D2C ecommerce. **Subscription, loyalty, low discovery, frustration-free shippable packaging.**

This is the **smallest-revenue, highest-margin** lane. Pre-launch audience-build (Ishan's afternoon ask, Block 12) probably *seeds* this tier — early loyalists buying online before Walmart launches — but it's not where the brand makes money.

D2C also gives the brand a **direct creative and email channel** (Chase Dimond's territory) for the audience to live in *between* mass-retail buying events. That's strategically valuable even if commercial scale is small.

## The "hybrid = conventional grocery?" workshop question

The deck flags an open question on slide 11:

> "To be brainstormed:
> - Hybrid = conventional grocery? (i.e. primary household weekly shop = proximity bias + mid-frequency + less price driven)
> - Common implications? (e.g. eye-level merchandising)"

**Translation:** Where does the *conventional grocery* shopper sit — the Kroger / Albertsons / Publix weekly shopper who isn't doing a Walmart price-stockup OR a 7-Eleven impulse-grab? They're somewhere between Tier 1 and Tier 2. The deck asks if they're a hybrid worth modeling separately, or if Tier-1 packaging just travels to grocery with minor adjustments.

**Workshop call.** Likely answer: *yes, grocery is a Tier-1.5 lane,* but Tier 1 packaging mostly works; the variable is shelf placement (grocery freezer is more crowded, more competing premium SKUs).

## Strategic implications for the workshop

### Tier sequencing locks the SKU decisions
- **Hero Ice Pop = Tier 1 multi-pack.** That's the form factor the room is designing.
- **All other SKUs (fruit snacks, chip, chocolate milk) follow Tier 1 first.** Brand consistency across Tier 1 SKUs > Tier expansion within a single SKU.
- **Tier 2 single-serve is a 2027 conversation.** Not workshopped now.
- **Tier 3 nutritional-spec compliance** is a *formulation* conversation, not a brand-workshop one. Flag and park.
- **Tier 4 D2C** is the pre-launch's *audience-capture* lane (Block 12).

### Tier choice gates the aesthetic
- Tier 1 = **shelf-block visibility** is the design problem. Distinctive enough to find in a 6-foot freezer door; consistent enough to read as one brand at multi-pack scale.
- The reverse-mullet packaging principle is **Tier-1-optimized** — front for the kid (because the kid might be in the cart at Walmart), back for the parent (because the parent is doing the final shelf check).

### Tier choice gates the pre-launch audience-build (Block 12)
- Building an audience pre-launch in Tier 1 is hard — Walmart doesn't have a direct loyalty / discovery mechanism the way a D2C brand or even a 7-Eleven loyalty program does.
- Tier 4 (D2C) becomes the **pre-launch audience-capture engine** even if it's a small commercial channel. You sell to a pre-launch list, they post, the post seeds Tier-1 demand at Walmart shelf.
- This is the **Liquid Death playbook** — D2C-first to build cultural mass, then mass-retail to monetize at scale.

## What's NOT modeled in the deck

- **Dollar General / Dollar Tree.** The deck names Mass + Club + Discount as Tier 1 but doesn't separate the dollar-stores from Walmart. Different shopper math (often even tighter budget than Walmart core; older skew on grocery basket; different shelf depth). Probably a Tier-1.5 to flag.
- **Whole Foods / Sprouts / Trader Joe's.** Deck implicitly excludes these as "not our shopper." Confirmed — the brand is *not* a Whole Foods brand. But a *halo* SKU at Sprouts could be a 2027 conversation if Tier 1 proves out. Park.
- **Direct-to-school sampling programs.** Tier 3 (Captive) is contract-driven but there's a *pre-contract sampling* lane through PTA / Booster Club / Title-I-school partnerships. The deck doesn't flag. Could be a Block-12 lever.
