# 4 All Snacks — Personas Pack

**Source:** Ishan + Elliot strategy-session input deck, slides 12, 15-21. Captured 2026-05-18.

Eight named personas — six parents/grandparents (the buyer) + one dad (episodic buyer) + one tween-and-couple format. Each persona has **emotional resonance** (how they want to be spoken to), **functional targeting** (the marketing levers that move them), **brands worn** (their aesthetic ecosystem), and **content diet** (where to reach them).

This is the most important audience input the workshop has. Block 03 (Insights) reads from this. Block 06 (Brand Name) and Block 08 (Voice) stress-test against it — *can a single voice land Maria AND Megan?* That's the workshop question.

---

## Persona 1: "Only Option" Maria

### At-a-glance
Single mom, late 20s to late 30s, two kids ages 5 and 10. Exurban or working-class neighborhood, household income $25-40k, WIC/SNAP user.

Walmart isn't a *choice* for her — it's the only place in reach that stocks groceries, clothes, and school supplies under one roof at her price point. Her cadence is a monthly big trip aligned to benefits and paycheck timing plus weekly fill-ins for perishables.

Great Value across the board; name brands only when clearanced. Pediatric nutrition is heavily constrained — fortified cereal, peanut butter, frozen vegetables, milk.

**Why distinct:** She has no real choice set. Walmart's competitor for her isn't Target — it's going without.

### Brands worn
Nordstrom Rack · Gap Factory · Old Navy

### Content diet
CBS · FOX · NBC · ABC · Facebook · TikTok

### Emotional resonance
- **Resonant emotional framing:** "Good moms shop smart." Pride disguised as practicality. She is resourceful, not poor, and every message needs to mirror that self-image.
- **Aspirational brands:** Runs through the kids — she'll wear a clearance no-name top so her son can have Nike on his feet for school picture day.
- **What flops hard:** Charity-coded language, pity imagery, or premium-lifestyle aspirations she can't access. Functionally, she responds to per-unit price math, "more meals per pack" callouts, EBT/SNAP eligibility tags, and Rollback yellow signage.

### Functional targeting
- **Functional message framing:** Kids' health improvement, for life.
- **Constraints:** Need for money to go further pushes her toward fortified shelf-stable basics regardless of if her kid is 5 or 12.
- **Promotional:** Paper coupons still work for her — the Walmart app is unreliable on her phone and she doesn't trust it for digital coupons.
- **Biggest levers:** In-store end-of-aisle family-pack pricing with a visible price-per-serving readout. Or sampling that feeds her kids in the moment is disproportionately effective.

### Brand voice cues for Maria
- **DO:** Plain-language benefit, per-serving math on pack, EBT/SNAP signaling at shelf, "good moms shop smart" pride frame.
- **DON'T:** Charity-coded "for families in need" language. Premium-aspirational lifestyle imagery. Wellness performance.

## Persona 2: "30-Mile" Donna

### At-a-glance
Rural mom, 35-50, three kids spread across the 4-14 range. Lives in a small town 30+ miles from any other big-box, husband in construction, ag, oil services, HHI $55-80k.

Walmart is the commercial and social center of her county. Monthly stockup of $400-600 plus weekly milk-and-bread runs.

Single trips combine ammo, fishing gear, kid clothes, prescriptions, and a week of groceries.

Strong loyalty to American legacy brands.

**Why distinct:** Distance and category breadth. The competitive set is "drive an hour" or "do without," not another retailer.

### Brands worn
Wrangler · Carhartt · Justin Boots

### Content diet
Fox News · Facebook · AM Radio (#DependOnAM)

### Emotional resonance
- **Resonant emotional framing:** "Built tough," "made in America" when actually true, and family/community imagery that doesn't feel scripted. Emotional core is self-reliance and traditional values. She doesn't want to be sold to; she wants to be treated as capable.
- **Aspirational brands:** Already wearing them.
- **What flops hard:** Anything that reads urban, coastal, condescending, or coded against her cultural values bounces immediately.

### Functional targeting
- **Functional message framing:** No behavior change upgrade.
- **Constraints:** Anti-institutional biases limit health literacy.
- **Promotional:** Print circulars and shelf tags carry more weight than app pings; she still reads the Sunday flyer cover to cover.
- **Biggest levers:** Multi-pack value math and bundled shopping benefits.

### Brand voice cues for Donna
- **DO:** Built-tough plain language. Made-in-America when it's actually true. Print circular ad placement. Shelf tags. Multi-pack value math.
- **DON'T:** Urban-coastal lifestyle imagery. Condescending "let us help you understand nutrition" framing. App-only promotions.

## Persona 3: "Pickup-Order" Jess

### At-a-glance
Suburban dual-income mom, early 30s to early 40s, two kids ages 7 and 11. HHI $90-130k.

She also shops Target, Costco, and sometimes Whole Foods, but she uses Walmart+ for predictable bulk basics (paper goods, water, cleaning supplies, dry pantry) and for kid items where price-over-brand makes sense (socks, gym shorts, lunchbox snacks). Almost never enters a store — it's curbside and delivery.

**Why distinct:** She's running Walmart as one channel in a multi-retailer portfolio, optimized by category. Convenience and price extraction, not necessity.

### Brands worn
Athleta · Madewell · Allbirds

### Content diet
Instagram · Apple Podcasts · NYT app

### Emotional resonance
- **Resonant emotional framing:** Smart-trade-off: she's not budgeting, she's optimizing. Emotional core is "I am too efficient to be a Walmart shopper in the old sense." Walmart has to feel like a deliberate, intelligent channel choice for bulk basics — not a default, not a necessity.
- **Aspirational brands:** Vuori · lululemon · Alo · Hoka
- **What flops hard:** Heritage/Americana messaging, in-store experience copy (she's never inside), or any imagery that suggests she's price-constrained.

### Functional targeting
- **Functional message framing:** Efficiency — your existing snack is now more nutritious.
- **Constraints:** In-store mechanics are nearly irrelevant — the parking lot and the app are the entire interaction.
- **Promotional:** Walmart+ exclusive pricing.
- **Biggest levers:** All app-based.

### Brand voice cues for Jess
- **DO:** Optimizer framing. Walmart+ app-first promotion. Crystal-clear ingredient + benefit math viewable on-app product detail page. Athleisure-adjacent aesthetic (clean, athletic, not cheap, not preachy).
- **DON'T:** Heritage Americana. In-store-only mechanics. Price-constrained imagery.

## Persona 4: "Multi-Gen" Garcias

### At-a-glance
Multi-generational Hispanic household, parents in their 30s, grandmother in the home, three or four kids ages 4-14. HHI $50-75k from two earners.

Saturday morning Supercenter trip is a family event, not a chore. The cart mixes Maseca, dried chiles, Jarritos, Mexican-brand cookies and crackers with mainstream brands the kids see on YouTube.

Larger basket sizes than average, more produce, more meat by weight.

**Why distinct:** Shopping is a cultural-family activity rather than a transaction; basket composition reflects two food cultures coexisting, and the bilingual store experience and brand mix actively matter.

### Brands worn
Time and Tru · Wonder Nation · Nike

### Content diet
Univision · Telemundo · YouTube · TikTok · WhatsApp

### Emotional resonance
- **Resonant emotional framing:** Family unity and generosity; the Saturday Supercenter trip is a social event, not a chore. Multi-generational imagery (grandmother, parents, kids in one frame, not the standard nuclear-family stock photo), kids' education and aspirations.
- **Aspirational brands:** Jordans for the boys, American Eagle & Aerie for mom & girls.
- **What flops hard:** Tokenized cultural specificity.

### Functional targeting
- **Functional message framing:** Flavor profiles based on real food.
- **Constraints:** Broken or machine-translated Spanish reads worse than English.
- **Promotional:** Family-pack with warehouse-pack pricing.
- **Biggest levers:** Brand availability for staples the grandmother trusts alongside the kid-trend brands the kids see on YouTube. Weekend in-store sampling especially around back-to-school and quinceañera season.

### Brand voice cues for the Garcias
- **DO:** Real-food flavor profile (agua fresca, mango chamoy, tamarind). Multi-generational family imagery. Properly-translated Spanish if used at all (no machine translation). Saturday-trip activation timing.
- **DON'T:** Tokenized "Latin flavor" callouts. Spanglish as flavor branding (e.g., "El Mango" with English everywhere else). Single-mom-and-2-kids stock photography.

## Persona 5: "Raise Them Again" Pat

### At-a-glance
Retirement-age grandmother, 60-72, primary caregiver to one or two grandkids ages 4-12, often through informal kinship arrangements or formal kinship custody. Fixed income, Social Security plus maybe part-time work, HHI $25-45k.

Shops in-store, doesn't trust the app, prefers to see what she's buying.

Tends toward familiar brands she raised her own kids on (often outdated nutritionally) plus whatever the grandkids specifically request.

**Why distinct:** Generational gap in food culture, low digital adoption, and a different decision-making profile than "mom" — she's the caregiver but not the demographic most marketing is calibrated for. Often asset-rich and income-poor, which is its own pattern.

### Brands worn
JCPenney · Catherines · Easy Spirit

### Content diet
CBS · FOX · NBC · ABC · Fox News · Facebook

### Emotional resonance
- **Resonant emotional framing:** Wanting to do right by the grandkids combined with a real fear of being out of touch and a need to feel competent.
- **Aspirational brands:** Talbots for church.
- **What flops hard:** Trend language or anything that assumes she shops the way a 35-year-old does.

### Functional targeting
- **Functional message framing:** Simple, legible, and trusted (by an institution or organization with generational significance).
- **Constraints:** Pat is buying the cereals she trusted in 1985.
- **Promotional:** She's price-sensitive but will not trade dignity for a discount. Plain-language benefit explanations and no app-only pricing.
- **Biggest levers:** Store greeter is genuinely meaningful. Pharmacy as a trip anchor (refill → groceries → small gift for the grandkids is a very common sequence), large-print pricing, paper circulars, employee help availability (she will ask, and that interaction shapes loyalty), and layaway in season.

### Brand voice cues for Pat
- **DO:** Plain-language, large-print, institution-trusted benefit copy. In-store-only promotion. Newspaper-circular ad placement. Pharmacy-aisle adjacency (pharmacy gets her in the door).
- **DON'T:** Trend slang, app-only mechanics, shopping the way a 35-year-old does, dignity-trading discount frames.

## Persona 6: "Millennial" Megan

### At-a-glance
Millennial mom, 30-40, one or two kids ages 4-9, label-reading and health-conscious. Would shop Whole Foods if the budget allowed; it doesn't. HHI $60-90k.

Splits trips deliberately — Aldi for produce, eggs, dairy; Walmart for what Aldi doesn't carry (specific brands, household goods, kid apparel, Bentgo-style lunch supplies, the Great Value organic and Marketside lines).

She researches before she buys.

**Why distinct:** She's portfolio-shopping like Jess, but the optimization axis is ingredient quality and clean-label sourcing under a budget cap, not pure convenience.

### Brands worn
T.J. Maxx · Organic Great Value · Old Navy

### Content diet
Instagram · TikTok · NYT · Substack

### Emotional resonance
- **Resonant emotional framing:** Conscious-parent layered over real budget constraint, with persistent low-grade guilt about the gap between her aspirations and what she can actually buy.
- **Aspirational brands:** Vuori · Outdoor Voices · Madewell · Stasher
- **What flops hard:** Vague "natural" or "wholesome" claims (she reads the back of the box), cartoon mascots, kid-marketing tropes, and anything that pattern-matches to the processed-food marketing she's actively skeptical of.

### Functional targeting
- **Functional message framing:** Ingredient transparency, third-party certifications she actually trusts (USDA Organic, Non-GMO Project, MSC), and honest side-by-side organic-versus-conventional comparison.
- **Constraints:** If it's not legible, it's not trustworthy.
- **Promotional:** Parent-creator endorsements over celebrity ones. Marketside section prominence (she navigates straight to it), the Great Value Organic line with clear price-per-unit math against conventional, fresh produce quality signaling, and better-for-you endcaps. She trusts the shelf tag more than the circular.
- **Biggest levers:** Megan is optimizing micronutrient density at any age.

### Brand voice cues for Megan
- **DO:** Ingredient transparency front-of-pack (vitamin list, micronutrient density). Honest organic-vs-conventional comparison. Parent-creator UGC. Marketside / better-for-you endcap placement.
- **DON'T:** Vague "natural" or "wholesome." Cartoon mascots. Kid-marketing tropes that pattern-match to Big Food.

**⚠️ TENSION:** Megan and Donna want OPPOSITE things on pack. Megan wants legible nutrition density and ingredient transparency; Donna wants "built tough, made in America, kids love it." This is the **central voice/aesthetic tension** the workshop has to resolve. The reverse-mullet packaging principle is the deck's proposed solution — front-of-pack for Donna's kids + back-of-pack for Megan's reading.

## Persona 7: "Tween Run" Ron

### At-a-glance
Father, 40-50, kids 9-14, often older kids too. He doesn't do the household grocery shopping — that's someone else. He shows up at Walmart for back-to-school, Christmas, birthdays, gaming gift cards, sports equipment, athletic socks, the kid's growth-spurt jeans, and whenever he's volun*told*. HHI $50-100k.

In-and-out, mission-driven, Saturday or Sunday afternoon trip with the kid in tow.

**Why distinct:** Not the primary household shopper. Episodic, category-narrow trips. Walmart's competition here is Amazon, GameStop, and Dick's — not Kroger or Aldi.

### Brands worn
Levi's · Hanes · Athletic Works

### Content diet
Reddit · ESPN · Sports Talk 99.5 · YouTube

### Emotional resonance
- **Resonant emotional framing:** Speed and specificity are everything; he doesn't browse and resents being made to. However, he feels obligated — "I want to come through for my kid without looking like I don't know what I'm doing."
- **Aspirational brands:** Kids drive changes — Nike Tech, Peter Millar.
- **What flops hard:** Lifestyle parenting content, soft emotional messaging, anything that requires him to learn the category.

### Functional targeting
- **Functional message framing:** Better for your kid without any of the added hassle — be the nutritional hero they deserve, not the guy you feel like.
- **Constraints:** Lacks motivation to shop & learn.
- **Promotional:** Sense of urgency.
- **Biggest levers:** Sports partnerships.

### Brand voice cues for Ron
- **DO:** One-line benefit. Sports partnership signaling (Little League sponsor, AAU presence). Aisle-end placement near sports equipment / gaming. Time-pressured promotional copy ("today only," "weekend sale").
- **DON'T:** Long-form lifestyle parenting. Soft emotional messaging. Anything requiring category education.

## Persona 8: "Convenience" Kim + Cal

### At-a-glance
Working-class parents with hourly or shift jobs (nursing assistants, warehouse, retail) HHI $55-75k, two or three kids ages 4-12.

Trips happen whenever someone has time — often late evening or a weekend morning.

The basket mixes necessity with treat-trip behavior; kids come along, pick up a small toy or snack, the parents grab dinner staples plus heavy on packaged convenience foods because they're tired.

**Why distinct:** They're time-poor and budget-constrained simultaneously. Walmart wins on time-cost as much as price — they're not "only option" like Maria, but no other format competes for an 8pm Tuesday family run.

### Brands worn
Hanes · George · Old Navy

### Content diet
Netflix · YouTube Music · TikTok · Facebook · YouTube

### Emotional resonance
- **Resonant emotional framing:** "The kids will love this. Upgrade your after-dinner treat." They are exhausted but also feel real pride in providing despite the grind, plus a treat-yourself attitude at the end of a hard day or week.
- **Aspirational brands:** Kids first — Nike · Under Armour
- **What flops hard:** Long-form copy, prep-heavy meal ideas, premium clean-label messaging without a price match.

### Functional targeting
- **Functional message framing:** Price match math with more nutrients.
- **Constraints:** "Don't make me think."
- **Promotional:** Family-pack value.
- **Biggest levers:** Removing friction between them and their kids' needs.

### Brand voice cues for Kim + Cal
- **DO:** Treat-language framing. Family-pack value with one-line claim. Friction-removal copy ("ready in 0 minutes," "no prep"). Evening-purchase timing assumption.
- **DON'T:** Long-form copy. Meal-prep instructions. Premium clean-label without a price comparison.

## Cross-persona patterns

### Where they all align
- **All eight buy at Walmart.** Different reasons, different cadences, same shelf.
- **All eight care about their kids.** Different vocabularies, same underlying motivation.
- **All eight reject preachy wellness.** Even Megan — the most health-conscious — flops hard on "natural / wholesome" vague claims.
- **All eight respond to per-serving / per-pack price math** at shelf. Even Jess (who never enters the store) responds to it in app product detail.

### Where they diverge sharply
- **Maria + Donna vs. Jess + Megan** — store-presence vs. app-only. Two different *channels of contact* even at the same retailer.
- **Megan vs. Donna** — ingredient legibility vs. built-tough Americana. Opposite aesthetic codes.
- **Garcias vs. all others** — multi-generational + bilingual + Saturday-as-event. Different *shopping social structure.*
- **Pat vs. all others** — generational gap, in-store loyalty, print-circular dependence. Different *information environment.*
- **Ron vs. all others** — episodic, mission-driven, sports-channel. Different *trip pattern.*
- **Kim + Cal vs. all others** — time-poverty + budget-constraint stacking. Different *cognitive bandwidth at purchase moment.*

### What this means for the brand voice
The brand has to land *one voice* across all eight. The deck's "We Are / We Are Not" already gestures at it:

- **WE ARE:** Approachable + plainspoken, bold/colorful/fun, innovative, "natural glycemic" / "clean sugar"
- **WE ARE NOT:** Wellness/health-foods brand, premium brand, full of gross ingredients

The voice that lands here is **plain-language confident.** Not wellness ("the optimal choice for your child's health"). Not Big Food ("kids love it!"). Something in between — *factual, warm, kid-respecting, parent-respecting, doesn't condescend, doesn't lecture.*

The closest existing-brand parallels (rough):
- **Liquid Death** (plain-language confident, bipartisan, loud-color, anti-wellness-coded)
- **De La Calle** (real-food flavor, Hispanic-crossover, bold packaging, mass-accessible)
- **Vacation** (loud-color, fun, retro warmth, accessible)
- **Bobbie / Cerebelly** (parent-respecting, plain-language nutrition, but premium-priced — what we're NOT)

McCabe + Hegarty will land the actual voice in Block 08.

## How this feeds the workshop

**Block 03 (Insights):**
The Garcias persona alone is a Steel/Sutherland Block — *what's the deprivation? what does the Saturday Supercenter family event look like without the brand vs. with it? what does it mean to a grandmother that her grandkid eats something she also approves of, in a category she doesn't trust?*

**Block 04 (Territories):**
Hegarty asks — what *zag* does this whole persona set invite? Probably *anti-organic-slop, pro-real-food, plainspoken, multi-generational, anti-wellness.* Liquid Death for kids' snacks, with the Hispanic-crossover flavor system and the reverse-mullet packaging.

**Block 06 (Brand Name):**
*Can Maria, Garcias, Megan, and Ron all say the name without it tasting wrong in their mouth?* That's the test.

- "**4 All**" — passes for Maria (inclusive, doesn't preach), Garcias (the *all* reads as family), Ron (short, no nonsense). Megan might find it generic. Pat would find it confusing without context.
- "**Natural Rascal**" — Maria might bounce (the *rascal* reads as upscale-coded, possibly white-coded). Megan would love it (the *natural* + the *rascal* signal "fun-but-clean"). Garcias might love the *rascal* (kid-mischief is universal). Ron wouldn't care. Donna would find it odd.
- A **third option** — what the room generates. Probably one of: a kid-character name, a Hispanic-crossover bilingual mark, a one-word verb (like "Pop!"), or a parent-direct claim ("Half Better" type).

**Block 08 (Voice):**
McCabe pressure-tests every line against *can this be said to Maria without sounding charity-coded? Can it be said to Megan without sounding Big-Food? Can it be said to Donna without sounding coastal?*

## What's NOT in the deck (persona gaps)

- **No "single dad" persona.** Single-mom is Maria. Single-dad as a primary buyer is a real and growing segment, particularly post-divorce. Not modeled.
- **No "stay-at-home dad" persona.** Cultural relevance in some urban / progressive markets. Not modeled.
- **No Asian-American persona.** Asian-American grocery patterns are very different (more cooking-from-scratch, more multi-retailer, lower frozen-snack usage). Probably not the launch market, but worth flagging.
- **No "lapsed Big-Food eater coming back to family snacking" persona.** Pat is the closest analog (grandmother re-entering the buyer role) but there's also a 35-45 dad/mom who used to be sugar-conscious and is now buying for the kid. Could be a future segment.
- **No "kid as direct buyer."** The 9-14 demo with disposable allowance money is increasingly a direct buyer at C-stores. This is the Tier-2 conversation (Audience Tiers doc), not Tier-1, so it's OK to defer — but flag.
