# 4 All Snacks — We Are / We Are Not

**Source:** Ishan + Elliot strategy-session input deck, slides 6-8. Captured 2026-05-18. Locked by founders pre-workshop.

The brand identity guardrails. These are *negative-space* commitments — what the brand stops itself from being is as load-bearing as what it commits to. Workshop respects this; doesn't relitigate. Block 06 (Brand Name), Block 07 (Aesthetic), and Block 08 (Voice) all run inside this perimeter.

---

## We are…

- ✅ **Approachable & plainspoken**
- ✅ **Bold, colorful, fun**
- ✅ **Innovative**
- ✅ **"Natural glycemic" / "clean sugar"**

## We are NOT…

- ❌ **A wellness / health-foods brand**
- ❌ **A premium brand**
- ❌ **Full of gross ingredients** (some artificial is OK for cost)

---

## What this means in practice

### "Approachable & plainspoken"
- Voice doesn't perform expertise. Doesn't lecture.
- Reads to a 5th-grade-and-up reading level.
- No jargon (no *macros*, no *anti-inflammatory*, no *adaptogenic*).
- Reference voices that work: Liquid Death's deadpan, Vacation's warm-retro confidence, De La Calle's plain food-talk, Trader Joe's flyer voice.

### "Bold, colorful, fun"
- Loud-wall packaging on the freezer door (Davis's call ref: stack a 6-foot row of these and it should read as one brand).
- Front-of-pack permission to use real color, character, energy.
- "Fun" in the *Vacation / Liquid Death / De La Calle* register — adult-permission-coded fun, not Disney-coded fun. (Kids are not the only consumer; a large share of Otter Pops are eaten by parents — observed; sourcing TBD.)
- "Fun" is NOT *Squeeze It 90s gimmick fun.* Different tone — more crafted, more confident, less novelty.

### "Innovative"
- Different innovation per SKU is OK; *consistency of innovation across the portfolio* is the rule (deck slide 24).
- Pop = packaging twist-off + vitamin formulation + flavor lexicon update.
- Future SKUs will each carry their own innovation thesis — but together they read as "this brand keeps moving."
- "Innovative" doesn't mean *novel for novelty's sake* — it means *each SKU does something the incumbent doesn't.*

### "Natural glycemic" / "clean sugar"
- This is the **formulation language** the brand prefers — sugars sourced from real fruit / honey / lower-glycemic-load sweeteners.
- It's *NOT* "no sugar." Sugar stays. The story is *what kind of sugar* and *what's around it* (vitamins, fiber, electrolytes).
- Distinct from the "sugar-free / stevia / monk fruit" wellness-coded lane.

---

## What we are NOT (deeper)

### NOT a wellness / health-foods brand
- No green-and-white packaging.
- No leaf icons, no "wellness" branding system, no fonts that read as Whole Foods, Sprouts, Erewhon.
- The "free-from" claims stack on back-of-pack is *small,* not the visual hierarchy.
- Reference *negative* aesthetics: Honest Kids, The Daily Crave Organic Veggie Straws, Good Pop, anything that looks like it's trying to be in Whole Foods.

### NOT a premium brand
- **Same shelf, same price** as Otter Pops / Welch's / Gushers. This is a *hard* commitment.
- No "artisanal," no "small-batch," no "craft" language.
- No matte black + minimalist serif typography.
- No price cues that signal premium (no oversized clean labels with white space, no European-cafe aesthetic).
- Reference *negative* brands: Bobbie, Cerebelly, Once Upon a Farm, Kencko, RXBar Kids.

### NOT full of gross ingredients (some artificial is OK for cost)
- The deck's formulation thesis: *we'll keep sugar, we'll keep some artificial flavor or color where it's the difference between same-price-as-Otter-Pop and double-the-price, we'll add vitamins and lower the glycemic load and reformulate the worst ingredients.*
- This is **10% better, not 100% clean.** That's the parity model — *price parity* requires accepting some artificial. *Behavior parity* requires it tasting like the kid expects.
- The deck rejects the *clean-label dogma* of the better-for-you category. Real fruit juice claims at 2%? Not us. But also: 30% lower sugar with vitamins added at the same price as a Welch's pouch? Yes us.

---

## The elevator (deck verbatim)

> **10% better nutrition + tasty af = habit forming food = compounding health return!**

This is the **math equation** the brand runs on. It's not the brand voice — but it's the *operating thesis* every product, claim, and creative decision should be tested against.

- "10% better nutrition" → substantiated (see `07_Research/03_Nutritional_Gaps_USDA_2025.md`)
- "tasty af" → enforced by the *taste = jury & judge → performance parity* belief
- "habit forming food" → the Duhigg cue × routine × reward loop (see `07_Research/01_Category_Observations.md`)
- "compounding health return" → the long-arc framing for parents/doctors/teachers

---

## How this feeds the workshop

The workshop **never relitigates** these commitments. The room can debate *how* to express them (the name, the voice, the aesthetic, the packaging system) but not *whether* the brand is wellness-coded or premium-coded or clean-label-dogmatic. Those decisions are *done.*

This makes the workshop **faster.** The audit (Block 02) starts not from "what's wrong with your strategy?" but from "you've already locked the perimeter; we're going to fill in what's inside it."

---

## The workshop has to translate this into a Big Idea

The elevator math is *true* but it's not *organizing.* It tells a doctor or a nurse what the brand does. It doesn't tell the brand what to make next, what tone to write in, what shelf-system to design.

The workshop's central question (Block 05 Big Idea):

> *What's the ONE thought that lets us be all of these things — approachable, bold, innovative, natural-glycemic, NOT-wellness, NOT-premium, NOT-clean-dogmatic — and ties Ice Pop SKU #1 to Fruit Snack SKU #2 to Chip SKU #3 in a way every doctor, nurse, teacher, parent says back unprompted?*

That's the lock.
