## THE 3 CONTENDERS (post-WHY-lock pass)

### ⭐ **BIG DAYS** *(Davis's favorite — Round 1)*

**Davis verbatim:** *"Small snacks, big days. Every day is a big day for a kid. The small choices compound. That's kinda fun."*

| Dimension | Read |
|---|---|
| **Why it lands** | Lands the FI-3 compounding thesis in the name itself — every snack is a small choice; every day is a big day; the compounding across childhood IS the brand promise. *Big Days* is also kid-vocabulary ("today was a big day") + parent-vocabulary ("she had a big day at school"). |
| **Wordmark direction** | Editorial Serif (Tiempos register) — *BIG DAYS* set in heavy weight, top-left consistent position. Coral signal in wordmark; could play *DAYS* slightly different weight for emphasis. |
| **Manifesto opener fit** | *"For the parent who noticed. Big Days."* Carries the warmth + closes on the brand mark cleanly. |
| **SKU-line extension** | Big Days Pops · Big Days Fruit Snacks · Big Days Chips · Big Days Chocolate Milk. Reads consistent. Travels Kinder-shape (D-4). |
| **Tagline pairing** | *Small bites. Big win. Big Days.* — three-beat lockup; the brand mark IS the close of the tagline. |
| **Risk** | "Big" is slightly generic-coded (Big Cheese, Big Win, Big League). The *Days* suffix is the differentiator; without it, it's a category word. Trademark risk on *Big Days* in food category needs Saul check. |

### ⭐ **LITTLE WINS** *(Elliot's pick — Round 1)*

**Elliot verbatim:** *"I like Little Wins. People wanna fucking win."* Davis agreed.

| Dimension | Read |
|---|---|
| **Why it lands** | Echoes the locked tagline *SMALL BITES. BIG WIN.* directly — *Little Wins* is the tagline's plural-compounding form (many small wins → the big win compounded). Parent-vocabulary ("we'll take the little wins") + kid-vocabulary ("I won!"). |
| **Wordmark direction** | Editorial Serif + Heavy Slab pairing — *LITTLE* in serif, *WINS* in display slab; or both in serif with weight contrast. More typographic personality than Big Days. |
| **Manifesto opener fit** | *"For the parent who noticed. Little Wins."* Slightly more emotional close than Big Days; "wins" carries Davis's locked *win* signature beat. |
| **SKU-line extension** | Little Wins Pops · Little Wins Fruit Snacks · Little Wins Chips · Little Wins Chocolate Milk. Reads consistent. *Little Wins Chocolate Milk* is the longest scan in the future portfolio — still legible. |
| **Tagline pairing** | *Small bites. Big win. Little Wins.* — the brand name is the *plural* of the tagline's *big win*; could feel either coherent or repetitive. Test in Phase 4 renders. |
| **Risk** | "Little Wins" exists as a common-noun phrase (used in self-help, sports, parenting content) — trademark clearance harder than for a more invented name. Also competes for distinctiveness against *Big Wins* (very close phonetically). |

### ⭐ **BIG WINS** *(Round 2 already-in-the-running flag)*

**Davis flagged at top of Round 2:** *"Big Wins as already in the running going into Round 2."* (Block 04.8 voice/copy exercise treated *Big Wins* as the working brand-name placeholder throughout.)

| Dimension | Read |
|---|---|
| **Why it lands** | Most direct echo of the locked tagline *SMALL BITES. BIG WIN.* — *Big Wins* is the plural of the tagline's payoff. Most coherent SMP-to-tagline-to-brand-name through-line. |
| **Wordmark direction** | Editorial Serif heavy + display slab pairing OR all-caps heavy slab dominant — *BIG WINS* reads loud on shelf; serif registers warmth. Wordmark could be heavier/louder than Big Days because the name itself is more declarative. |
| **Manifesto opener fit** | *"For the parent who noticed. Big Wins."* Strongest emotional close — *win* is Davis's locked signature beat. |
| **SKU-line extension** | Big Wins Pops · Big Wins Fruit Snacks · Big Wins Chips · Big Wins Chocolate Milk. Reads consistent. *Big Wins Pops* is the most playful SKU-line wordmark in the portfolio. |
| **Tagline pairing** | *Small bites. Big win. Big Wins.* — same plural-echo issue as Little Wins. *Small Bites. Big Win. — Big Wins.* could read as the brand actually *being* the big wins, which is a strong identity claim. |
| **Risk** | Common-noun phrase in trademark sense — needs Saul + outside IP lawyer clearance. Phonetically very close to *Little Wins* — picking one functionally rules out the other. |

## OPEN DECISION FOR DAVIS

**Pick one of three for the Joe pitch:** Big Days · Little Wins · Big Wins.

Quick comparison:

| Criterion | Big Days | Little Wins | Big Wins |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lands FI-3 compounding | ✅ explicit (days compound) | ✅ implicit (wins compound) | ✅ implicit (the compounded payoff) |
| Echoes locked tagline (*Small bites. Big win.*) | Side-by-side, no echo conflict | Direct echo (plural of *win*) — coherent or repetitive? | Direct echo (plural of *win*) — same Q |
| Kid-vocabulary | ✅ (*today was a big day*) | ✅ (*I won!*) | ✅ (*we win!*) |
| Parent-vocabulary | ✅ (*she had a big day*) | ✅ (*we'll take the little wins*) | ✅ (*the win every parent agrees on*) |
| Davis's emotional read | ⭐ favorite | second | flagged as already-in-running |
| Elliot's emotional read | — | ⭐ favorite | — |
| Trademark complexity | Medium | High (common phrase) | High (common phrase) |
| Distinctiveness | Most distinctive of three | Most echoey | Most echoey |

**My read:** **Big Days** has the cleanest distinctiveness + your strongest emotional pull + side-by-side coherence with the tagline (doesn't fight it). **Little Wins** has Elliot's lock and the strongest tagline echo. **Big Wins** is the working placeholder from voice exercise; same echo logic as Little Wins.

If the call is hard, two paths:
- **Defer the lock; pitch Joe with the shortlist** — *"Three names on the table, Big Days / Little Wins / Big Wins. We're locking IP clearance now; final lock pending Saul. Here's the rationale for each."*
- **Lock one now; carry the other two as backup** if IP clearance burns the lead.
