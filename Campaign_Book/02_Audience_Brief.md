## CONTEXT

The Strategy Brief locked the brand POV: *Raising a kid is hard enough without negotiating snacks. We're rebuilding the snacks you used to grab — for the kids whose entire childhood you still get to shape. Same shelf. Same price. A win for the kid. A win for the parent. A win for the family.*

That POV has to land for **four launch-tier personas simultaneously,** at the Walmart freezer door, at $5.99 parity with Otter Pops, without tribal-coding any of them. This brief is the human portrait of those four — written so every downstream brief (Comms, Creative, Identity) knows exactly who's on the other end of the message.

---

## WHO WE'RE TALKING TO

The launch-tier set is locked: **Maria, Donna, the Garcias household, and Kim + Cal.** Selected from the 8-persona pack because they all share the same shelf moment — *Tier 1 Value Stock-Up at Walmart, multi-pack, planned provisioning* — and together they cover the four behavioral corners the brand has to span:

| Persona | Behavioral position |
|---|---|
| **Maria** | Top-left — Walmart-only, heritage-leaning, in-store, stockup, solo |
| **Donna** | Top-left — heritage-anchored, in-store, stockup, rural |
| **Garcias** | Middle — family-group, multi-generational, multi-cultural |
| **Kim + Cal** | Middle — family-group, time-poor + budget-constrained, treat-trip |

The other four personas in the pack (Jess, Pat, Megan, Ron) matter for **content amplification + secondary reach** — covered briefly at the bottom of this brief — but the basket math + voice gate runs through these four.

## THE HUMAN TRUTH THEY CAN'T SAY OUT LOUD

If you sat down with Maria, Donna, the Garcias parents, and Kim + Cal separately and asked *"what do you actually want from a kid-snack brand,"* none of them would answer the same way. Maria would talk price-per-unit. Donna would talk built-tough trust. Garcias would talk Saturday-cart-fit. Kim + Cal would talk 8pm-Tuesday-friction.

But if you sat under the kitchen-table conversation each is having with themselves — the one they don't put words to — you'd hear the same sentence under all four:

> **"I'm tired of being asked to choose. I want the snack to just be good. At the price I'm already paying. In the aisle I'm already shopping. Without the fight."**

They've been forced to choose for fifteen years between *cheap-fun-and-bad-for-them* (Otter Pops, Capri-Sun, Gushers) and *expensive-virtuous-and-the-kid-won't-eat-it* (Honest, Annie's, Once Upon a Farm). They've stopped pretending the choice is fair. **They opted out of the choice.** They're buying Otter Pops because the kid will eat it, the cashier won't judge them, and the budget can take it — and they're quietly aware they shouldn't have to settle.

**The brand's permission to speak comes from saying: *we noticed the same thing you did. We removed the choice. Here it is.*** That's the unlock. Territory A — *The Parent Who Noticed.* This brief is the four faces of that parent.

## THE 4 LAUNCH-TIER PERSONAS

### "Only-Option" Maria

**Who she is.** Late-20s to late-30s single mom, two kids ages 5 and 10, exurban or working-class neighborhood, $25-40k household income, WIC/SNAP user. Walmart isn't a *choice* for her — it's the only place in reach that stocks groceries, clothes, and school supplies under one roof at her price point. Cadence: monthly big trip aligned to benefits + paycheck timing, plus weekly fill-ins for perishables. Great Value across the board; name brands only when clearanced.

**The internal monologue.** *"Good moms shop smart."* She is resourceful, not poor — and every message that mirrors that self-image lands. Every message that treats her as a charity case bounces off. She knows her constraints; she doesn't want them named back to her.

**Drivers to purchase:**
- Per-unit price math visible on shelf or pack — *"X cents per pop"*
- EBT/SNAP eligibility tag
- In-store sampling that feeds her kids in the moment (disproportionately powerful — Maria is the persona most moved by *immediate kid-satisfaction* at the point of purchase)
- Rollback yellow signage / clearance tags
- Multi-pack value math ("more meals per pack")

**Barriers:**
- Charity-coded language ("for families in need")
- Pity imagery
- Premium-aspirational lifestyle she can't access
- Wellness performance ("the optimal choice for your child's health")
- App-only promotions (Walmart app unreliable on her phone; she doesn't trust digital coupons)
- Anything that makes her trip more cognitively expensive — she's already running a complex weekly calculation

**Where to reach her:**
- **In-store first.** End-of-aisle family-pack pricing with visible price-per-serving readout. Endcap during summer. Sampling tables.
- **Paper coupons** + Sunday circular ads (still work for her even though the rest of the world is digital).
- **TikTok + Facebook** for awareness reach — but expect zero clickthrough.
- **EBT/SNAP signaling** at shelf — small text, big trust signal.

**Voice cues — DO:**
- Plain-language benefit
- Per-serving math on pack
- "Good moms shop smart" pride frame
- Multi-pack value forward
- EBT/SNAP signaling

**Voice cues — DON'T:**
- Charity-coded "for families in need"
- Premium-aspirational lifestyle imagery
- Wellness performance
- App-first mechanics
- Long-form copy

---

### "30-Mile" Donna

**Who she is.** Rural mom, 35-50, three kids spread across 4-14, lives in a small town 30+ miles from any other big-box. Husband in construction, ag, or oil services. HHI $55-80k. Walmart is the commercial *and social* center of her county. Monthly stockup of $400-600 plus weekly milk-and-bread runs. Single trips combine ammo, fishing gear, kid clothes, prescriptions, and a week of groceries. Strong loyalty to American legacy brands.

**The internal monologue.** *"I don't need to be lectured. I need stuff that works."* Self-reliance is her central emotional pillar. She doesn't want to be sold to — she wants to be treated as capable. She's also quietly aware that the wellness aisle is "for people not like me," and she's not asking permission to enter it.

**Drivers to purchase:**
- *Built tough* plain language
- *Made in America* claims **when actually true** (she sniffs out fake patriotism instantly)
- Multi-pack value math
- Bundled shopping benefits (snacks + lunchbox + sports drink in one trip)
- Family / community imagery that doesn't feel scripted
- Print circular ad placement + Sunday flyer
- Shelf tags she can read at a distance

**Barriers:**
- Anything that reads urban or coastal
- Condescending "let us help you understand nutrition"
- App-only promotions
- Anti-institutional framings she pattern-matches to coastal-elite voice
- Tribal coding either direction (the brand cannot align with her politics OR against them — bipartisan only)

**Where to reach her:**
- **Sunday print circular** + AM radio + Facebook
- **In-store** — endcap and freezer-door visual recognition
- **County fairs / sports-event sponsorship** in-person presence
- **Friend-of-friend recommendation** via community-tight WhatsApp/Facebook groups

**Voice cues — DO:**
- Built-tough plain language
- Made-in-America when true
- Print circular + shelf tags
- Multi-pack value math
- Plain-confident, never preachy

**Voice cues — DON'T:**
- Urban-coastal lifestyle imagery
- Condescending nutrition-education framing
- App-only promotions
- Tribal political coding

### "Multi-Gen" Garcias

**Who they are.** Multi-generational Hispanic household, parents in their 30s, grandmother in the home, three or four kids ages 4-14. HHI $50-75k from two earners. Saturday morning Supercenter trip is a **family event, not a chore.** The cart mixes Maseca, dried chiles, Jarritos, Mexican-brand cookies + mainstream brands the kids see on YouTube. Larger basket sizes than average; more produce, more meat by weight.

**The internal monologue.** *"I want my kid to have what other kids have, AND I want the grandmother to recognize what's in the cart, AND I want it to feel like us — not someone's idea of us."* Cultural respect is the gate; tokenization is the third rail.

**Drivers to purchase:**
- Real-food flavor profile (agua fresca, mango chamoy, tamarind — *Wave 2 territory; launch lineup stays mainstream*)
- Multi-generational family imagery — grandmother, parents, kids in one frame
- Brand availability for staples the grandmother trusts *alongside* kid-trend brands the kids see on YouTube
- Family-pack with warehouse-pack pricing
- Saturday in-store sampling, especially around back-to-school and quinceañera season
- Properly-translated Spanish *if used at all* — broken or machine-translated Spanish reads worse than no Spanish

**Barriers:**
- Tokenized cultural specificity
- Spanglish as flavor branding (e.g., *"El Mango"* with English everywhere else)
- Single-mom-and-2-kids stock photography
- Coastal-coded design language
- English-only with Spanish nowhere → reads as not-for-us

**Where to reach them:**
- **Univision / Telemundo** linear and digital
- **YouTube + TikTok** — kid-trend channels especially
- **WhatsApp** — primary intra-family + community channel
- **Saturday in-store sampling** + bilingual-staff demos
- **School + church bulletin** ads where relevant (Garcias-tier engagement is community-rooted)

**Voice cues — DO:**
- Real-food flavor language (mango chamoy, agua fresca, tamarind — Wave 2)
- Multi-gen family imagery
- Properly-translated Spanish when used (no machine translation)
- Saturday-trip activation timing
- Mainstream + cultural-resonance flavor pairing (Starburst Wild-Berry-vs-Tropical model)

**Voice cues — DON'T:**
- Tokenized "Latin flavor" callouts
- Spanglish flavor branding without bilingual context
- Single-mom-and-2-kids stock photography
- Coastal / boutique design coding

### "Convenience" Kim + Cal

**Who they are.** Working-class parents with hourly or shift jobs (nursing assistants, warehouse, retail), HHI $55-75k, two or three kids ages 4-12. Trips happen whenever someone has time — often late evening or weekend morning. The basket mixes necessity with **treat-trip behavior** — kids come along, pick up a small toy or snack, parents grab dinner staples + heavy on packaged convenience because they're tired.

**The internal monologue.** *"I'm tired. Don't make me think. The kid will love this and that's enough. The week was hard; let's give them a small treat."* They are exhausted but also feel real pride in providing despite the grind, plus a treat-yourself attitude at the end of a hard day or week.

**Drivers to purchase:**
- **Friction-removal copy** ("ready in 0 minutes," "no prep") — Kim/Cal is the persona where Davis's directive D-5 (*friction-removal explicit*) lands hardest
- **Treat-language framing** ("the after-dinner upgrade," "Friday-night snack")
- Family-pack value with one-line claim — they don't read paragraphs at 8pm Tuesday
- Removing the negotiation friction between them and their kids' needs
- Evening-purchase timing assumptions (the trip is at 8pm, not 11am)

**Barriers:**
- Long-form copy
- Prep-heavy meal ideas
- Premium clean-label messaging without a price match
- Anything that asks them to learn the category
- Anything that adds a decision

**Where to reach them:**
- **Late-evening Meta + TikTok** (Netflix-second-screen scroll)
- **YouTube Music** background play
- **In-store endcap** at 8pm during the trip itself
- **Friction-removal copy** at every touchpoint — the brand is the thing that doesn't add to their cognitive load

**Voice cues — DO:**
- Treat-language framing
- Family-pack value + one-line claim
- Friction-removal copy
- Evening-purchase timing
- "The kids will love this"

**Voice cues — DON'T:**
- Long-form copy
- Meal-prep instructions
- Premium clean-label without price comparison
- Wellness-aisle vocabulary

## CROSS-PERSONA TENSIONS THE BRAND DESIGNS AROUND

Three structural tensions the brand has to hold simultaneously. The reverse-mullet packaging principle is the deck's structural answer to all three.

### Tension 1: Maria + Donna (heritage, in-store, stockup) vs. Garcias + Kim/Cal (family-group, middle quadrant)

Top-left personas (Maria + Donna) are doing **planned provisioning** — the kid is often not in the cart, the parent does the final shelf check, the decision is rational + price-anchored. Middle-quadrant personas (Garcias + Kim/Cal) are doing **family-group shopping** — the kid points, the parent approves, the decision is distributed. **Both have to win at the shelf moment.** Front-of-pack works for both *only* if it serves the family-group kid-point + the solo parent's 2-second scan equally.

### Tension 2: Donna's heritage code vs. Megan-tier ingredient-transparency code

Donna wants *built-tough, made-in-America, kids-love-it.* Megan (secondary tier — content-amplifier) wants *ingredient legibility, third-party certifications, parent-creator endorsement.* These are **opposite codes on pack.** Front-of-pack can't serve both without one losing — so **back-of-pack carries the Megan-tier transparency** (ingredient-density, nutrition comparison, third-party callouts) while front-of-pack carries the Donna-tier confidence (loud color, brand wordmark, appetite cue). *Reverse-mullet.*

### Tension 3: Bipartisan-by-design across Maria + Donna + Garcias + Kim/Cal

The four launch-tier personas live in different political tribes, religious cultures, regional sensibilities, and aesthetic codes. **No tribal-coding either direction** — not coastal-elite, not MAHA-coded, not red-state-pandering, not Hispanic-specifically-coded. The brand acts as if these tribes don't exist in its voice — and serves all of them on shelf.

## HOW "NO BEHAVIOR CHANGE" LANDS PER PERSONA (Davis directive D-5)

Friction-removal as a strategic principle manifests differently for each persona:

| Persona | What "no behavior change" means for them |
|---|---|
| **Maria** | "Same trip. Same aisle. Same budget. Better what I put in the cart for the kid." |
| **Donna** | "Same Walmart run that includes ammo + groceries + kid clothes. New shelf-find, same flow." |
| **Garcias** | "Saturday family Supercenter trip stays Saturday family Supercenter trip. New SKU slots into the cart between Jarritos and Capri-Sun. Grandmother doesn't have to learn anything." |
| **Kim + Cal** | "8pm Tuesday last-minute run. One less decision to make at the freezer door. Kid happy. Done." |

This is **the brand's central operational kindness** — it doesn't ask the parent to learn anything new, change anything, or pay anything more. It just slots in where they already are.

## SECONDARY PERSONAS — context for content + reach (not launch-tier basket math)

The other four personas matter for *content amplification + secondary reach*. They're not the basket math, but the brand has to be coherent across them or it loses cultural authority.

| Persona | Why they matter |
|---|---|
| **"Pickup-Order" Jess** *(suburban dual-income, $90-130k, multi-retailer, app-first)* | **Content amplifier.** She'll see the brand on Instagram, talk about it in her group chat, and validate-by-proxy for her network. App-first product-detail-page copy + creator-led discovery wins her. |
| **"Raise-Them-Again" Pat** *(grandmother caregiver, $25-45k, in-store, heritage)* | **Secondary launch buyer.** Buys the brands she trusted in 1985 + whatever the grandkid asks for. Plain-language, large-print, paper-circular. Store greeter + pharmacy adjacency = trip anchor. |
| **"Millennial" Megan** *(label-reader, $60-90k, Aldi+Walmart split)* | **Content amplifier + ingredient-transparency lens.** She'll read every label, post the comparison on Substack, and either champion or burn the brand. Ingredient legibility + parent-creator UGC + no vague "wholesome" claims. |
| **"Tween-Run" Ron** *(tween dad, episodic mission, sports/games)* | **Sports-channel relevance.** Doesn't do household groceries but shows up for sports gear / Christmas / back-to-school. Reachable via Little League / AAU sponsorship + sports-equipment aisle adjacency. |

**Strategic implication:** Comms Brief budgets primary reach for launch-tier 4; allocates a smaller bucket for secondary-tier content-amplification (Jess + Megan in particular drive cultural credibility that loops back to launch-tier trust).

## MANDATORIES & CONSIDERATIONS (carried + new)

Carried from Strategy Brief:

- **V-1 Positive-POV only.** Brand speaks from its own block. Maria + Donna + Garcias + Kim/Cal hear no *"wellness left,"* no *"unlike X,"* no *"finally a brand that."*
- **V-2 Benefit not feature.** *"100% Vitamin C"* burns; *"won't crash at 2pm"* wins.
- **V-3 No "ritual" / aspirational vocabulary.** Concrete, kid-anchored, grounded.
- **V-4 No implicit comparisons.** Comparative trigger words (*better, more, less than*) burn unless they refer to the brand's own block.
- **P-1 Reverse-mullet packaging.** Front = kid + parent-scan; back = parent-flip + Megan-tier transparency.
- **P-2 Kid-joy gate.** Never compromise the fun.

New audience-specific mandatories:

- **M-1 Bipartisan-by-design across all four launch-tier personas.** No political coding either direction.
- **M-2 Cross-cultural without tokenization.** Garcias' multi-generational household read should always have *grandmother present in frame,* and Spanish (when used) must be properly translated — never machine-translated, never Spanglish-branded.
- **M-3 In-store sampling is a load-bearing mechanic** for Maria + Garcias specifically (free trial = zero loss = breaks the trial-risk equation).
- **M-4 Friction-removal is the universal voice rule**, not a Kim/Cal-only register. It just *lands hardest* for Kim/Cal.
- **M-5 Front-of-pack must serve both family-group point AND solo parent 2-second scan.** Single design surface, two reading modes.
