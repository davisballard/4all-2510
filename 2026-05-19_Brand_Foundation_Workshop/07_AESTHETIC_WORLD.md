### Goodles coastal-coding refinement (added live in Block 03 C2)

Elliot flagged in Block 03: **Goodles is a coastal brand.** Launched in San Francisco as a California brand — clearly meant for coastal parents. 4 All Snacks is for the **mass-retail value shopper** (Maria, Donna, Garcias, Kim+Cal) — *not* coastal. **Extract Goodles' mechanics, drop its positioning.**

- ✅ Take the **multi-mode interaction** ("looks good / looks fun / looks interesting — flip for receipts")
- ✅ Take the **don't-lead-with-health** discipline (front = appetite + curiosity; health goes on the side)
- ✅ Take the **clearly-new + clearly-not-shitty** visual freshness (Goodles vs Kraft, the action-spur Ishan locked)
- ❌ Drop the SF/California signal (boutique-coastal type system, soft pastels read-by-design-school, "founder-letter-on-back" voice)
- ❌ Drop *anything* that pattern-matches to coastal-organic register

Visual register has to land in Yuma and Tulsa and Macon, not just Brooklyn and Berkeley. **De La Calle is the closer geographic template** (Mexican-cultural-coded, ships mass-tier) — Liquid Death is the *attitude* template — Goodles is the *interaction-layer* template — Mighty Fundippa is the *packaging-architecture* template. Four references, four jobs.

### The visually-boring-brown anti-pattern (Ishan, Block 03 Cross-Persona Set #1, named gate)

> *"Other brands are taking stuff out, not adding stuff. Nabisco / Kraft Heinz no-sugar / no-dye SKUs — packaging is **literally brown** because they're trying to convey natural. **This is so shitty.** Designers convey 'better-for-you' through visual minimalism = visually boring. **We can't fall into that trap.**"*

→ **Top-line anti-pattern for this block.** Better-for-you signaling has to come through **color, freshness, fun, and visible appetite-cue** — NOT through:

- ❌ Beige / cream / brown / earthtone color palette
- ❌ Minimalist no-claim-no-illustration negative-space pack
- ❌ "Trying to look natural" by *removing* visual interest
- ❌ Stacked free-from claims as the primary front-of-pack message
- ❌ Premium-coded-restrained design that reads as "expensive even when priced parity"

✅ **What we do instead:** Saturated color · real-food appetite cue · clearly-new visual freshness · *better-for-you signaled through density of the food signal,* not through the absence of stuff. **Goodles' mac-and-cheese illustration is the model** — it shows the food being delicious AND lets the side carry the receipts.

### Cluttered-front anti-pattern (Block 03 Set #1 Q2 100% hit)

> *"It has to be a classic. They see it — no analysis paralysis — they just go 'alright, my kids will eat this.'"* — Ishan

→ **Disqualifier #2 was 100%-confirmed: 'cluttered' burns trust harder than any other signal.** Block 07 front-of-pack discipline: **maximum 3 things compete for attention.** Flavor cue + brand wordmark + one benefit anchor. The rest goes to the side. *Cognitive-fluency principle* (Shotton): the pack has to be parseable in <1 second.

### Loss-aversion as design adversary (added live in Block 03 C2)

Ishan + Elliot named **fear-of-regret-on-trial** as a real consumer truth for the mass-retail value shopper. The packaging has to engineer **four touchpoints of non-disappointment** (see `11_IDENTITY_PACKAGING.md` for the framework). For Block 07 specifically — the *shelf-pickup moment* is the first non-disappointment moment, which means visual register must signal **"premium quality, legit, established"** *without* signaling **"premium price."** Goodles does this through "established 1946" cues; 4 All Snacks does it through whatever this block locks. *Feeling legit in hand* is the brief.

## TYPOGRAPHY LANE

**MASSIMO:**
> "The deck uses a serif for headlines and a sans for body. The serif feels Tiempos-or-similar — editorial, warm-confident, not premium-cold. That's a *strong* deck-level call that the brand should consider keeping.
>
> Three typographic registers we could land in:
>
> **Register A — Editorial Serif** (e.g., Tiempos, Domaine, GT Sectra, or a similar contemporary high-contrast serif)
>
> - Warmth + confidence + plainspoken authority.
> - Reads *editorial,* not *fashion.* Not Vogue. More like the New York Times Magazine or the Trader Joe's flyer at scale.
> - Pairs with a clean grotesque sans (Inter, Söhne, ABC Diatype) for body copy.
> - Risk: could read premium if not handled carefully. The trick is heavy weights + tight kerning + bold color blocking. Tiempos Black at 60pt on a coral background reads *kid-friendly-confident,* not *Bottega-Veneta-premium.*
>
> **Register B — Heavy Display Slab** (e.g., Cooper Black, Heavyweight Pixar-coded display)
>
> - Plenty of kid-snack permission. Vacation uses this lane. Liquid Death's marketing material flirts with it.
> - Reads loud, kid-friendly, warm-retro.
> - Risk: dated. Cooper Black has been having a moment for five years; the moment could end. Hard to scale across SKUs as the only voice.
>
> **Register C — Mixed Custom** (a custom display face for the brand name + neutral grotesque for body)
>
> - The brand commissions its own type for the wordmark + headline tier. Body in a serviceable workhorse.
> - Most defensible long-term; most expensive short-term.
> - Risk: custom type can age badly if the brand world shifts.
>
> ***Distinctiveness data (research):*** *Across 18 surveyed kid-snack brands at the Walmart freezer-pop tier, **no brand uses editorial display serif as primary wordmark.** Annie's brushes it (extra-bold rounded serif) but stays warmth-coded, not editorial-confidence. The lane is genuinely empty. See `02_Visual/02_Competitor_Visual_Landscape.md`.*
>
> **My push:** Register A — Editorial Serif. The deck is already pointing there. It fits *For the parent who noticed* — the brand respects the parent enough to use a serif that takes itself seriously without being premium. It's also the *least common* visual move at Walmart freezer-snack tier, which is mostly display slabs and rounded sans-serifs. Distinctiveness on shelf."

**FINK:**
> "I'll co-sign Register A but with a thumb on Register B for emergencies. The brand can do *Editorial Serif as primary, Heavy Slab as accent.* Tiempos Black for *YES* (if Yes Snacks wins) or *4All* (if 4All wins); Cooper-style slab for flavor-name callouts on pack. Two-register typography system: one editorial, one playful."

**GIUSEPPE:**
> "Both registers work for AI-generated kinetic text in ads. Editorial Serif animates with confidence (text scales up from the bottom, holds, fades). Heavy Slab animates with bounce (text scales in with a bounce-out curve). The combination gives us *two kinetic registers* for two emotional beats — confident statement + playful kid-payoff."

## COLOR SYSTEM

**FINK:**
> "Color call. Three approaches:
>
> **A — Single brand color + color-per-flavor accents.** Coral red as the brand color (from the deck). Each Ice Pop flavor gets a secondary color treatment within the coral master frame. Stacked at shelf, the freezer reads as a *coral wall with flavor pops within it.*
>
> **B — Color-per-SKU as the brand system.** No master brand color. Each SKU lives in its own color world (Strawberry-Passionfruit in pink, Mango-Chamoy in orange, Watermelon-Agua-Fresca in teal). Stacked at shelf, reads as a *rainbow.* (Vacation does this.)
>
> **C — Hybrid.** Coral red as a small unifying brand mark across SKUs; each SKU dominated by its flavor color. Stacked at shelf, reads as a *rainbow with one consistent coral signal* (the wordmark or brand mark).
>
> The Big Idea recommendation: **C — Hybrid.** Loud-wall shelf strategy *wants* the rainbow effect — each color contributes to the wall. But brand-recognition *wants* the unifying signal. Hybrid wins both.
>
> Practical: each SKU is dominated by its flavor color (90%); the brand mark / wordmark is in coral or in a consistent secondary color that travels across SKUs (10%).
>
> Stress-test: 'reverse mullet' rule — does this work on front and back?
>
> Front of pack: flavor color dominates, kid-targeted visual loudness.
> Back of pack: cream / warm-white background with three benefit shields in three accent colors (per Mighty Fundippa precedent). Cream-on-back consistent across SKUs = parent-recognition signal.
>
> ***Cream-back data (research):*** *Across 18 surveyed kid-snack brands, every single one uses bright-white or saturated-color as background. **Cream/warm-off-white is genuinely unoccupied** as a parent-recognition system signal. See `02_Visual/02_Competitor_Visual_Landscape.md`.*
>
> Coral as the unifying signal: small wordmark front + reverse-mullet *shield-color* on back (one of the three benefit shields is coral). That's where the brand color *quietly* unifies the shelf."

**MASSIMO:**
> "Approve. The cream-on-back as parent-recognition is a sophisticated move. Maria's eye scans the freezer; she sees rainbow at kid-eye-level; her own eye flicks down and finds cream backs with cream legibility. *The cream is the brand.* Front is the SKU. Back is the brand."

**GIUSEPPE:**
> "In motion / ads — the rainbow renders well in social-stack vertical formats. SKU pop, brand wordmark hold, cream back-of-pack reveal. Three-beat kinetic structure."

## CHARACTER CALL

**FINK:**
> "Character is the third lock. Three options:
>
> **A — No character.** Typography-led brand. De La Calle does this. Vacation does this. The brand's personality lives in the type + color + tone. Most scalable long-term; least *immediate* kid-connection.
>
> **B — One mascot.** Mighty Fundippa-style. Single character anchors all SKUs. Tony the Tiger / Cap'n Crunch / Cheez-It Crew lineage. Strong kid-recognition; risks dating; requires character-development investment.
>
> **C — Cast of characters.** Smarties / Powerpuff Girls / Little Rascals lineage. Different characters per SKU or per flavor or per format. Most kid-engagement; highest production cost; risk of character-bloat.
>
> The Big Idea cuts against B and C. The Big Idea is *For the parent who noticed* — a parent-anchored, plainspoken POV brand. A *mascot* could undercut that. Mighty Fundippa's mascot is friendly but cartoony-coded; that's not the register we want.
>
> The deck doesn't push hard on character either way. The reverse-mullet reference visual (Mighty Fundippa) has a mascot, but the deck doesn't say *we want a mascot.*
>
> **My push:** Option A — No character (typography-led brand). The brand's personality lives in *the type, the color, the voice on pack, the brand mark.* If kid-connection is needed beyond that, it lives in the *flavor names* (*Mango Chamoy* is a character of its own — has personality, swagger, kid-appeal). No persistent mascot."

**MASSIMO:**
> "I support no character. Character brands age. Typographic brands compound."

**GIUSEPPE:**
> "Disagree slightly. *No character* loses an enormous content lever — the brand has no native voice in social, no person to feature in ads, no figure for kids to relate to. The brand becomes *the type and the pack* — sterile in motion.
>
> Counter-proposal: **Option D — Implied character via voice, not via illustration.** The brand has a *voice* (McCabe's manifesto and copy) but no illustration mascot. *The Brand* is the character — its tone, its language, its swagger. Liquid Death does this — there's no mascot, but the brand has a *personality.* Could be a sustainable middle path."

**FINK:**
> "Option D is what I meant. Typography + voice = brand personality. No drawn character.
>
> Locked: **no illustrated mascot. Brand personality lives in voice + type + color.**"

## PACKAGING SYSTEM PRINCIPLE (REVERSE MULLET CODIFIED)

**FINK:**
> "Codify the reverse-mullet rule. Front of pack = kid territory. Back of pack = parent territory. We've referenced this throughout the deck and the brand folder. Lock it as the *rule:*
>
> **FRONT (Kid Territory):**
>
> - Flavor color dominant (90%+ of pack)
> - Flavor name in display type (Heavy Slab register, all-caps or sentence-case)
> - Brand wordmark in fixed position (top-third, consistent across SKUs)
> - Pack format graphic (e.g., for Ice Pop: stylized pop with twist-off cap)
> - NO claims, NO free-from icons, NO supplement-facts text. Just flavor + brand.
> - Optional: pack ritual visual (e.g., kid holding pop, abstract movement graphic) — case-by-case.
>
> **BACK (Parent Territory):**
>
> - Cream/warm-white background, consistent across all SKUs
> - Three benefit shields, fixed grid (per Mighty Fundippa precedent), each shield with icon + one-word claim + one-sentence explanation
> - Supplement facts panel in regulatory-compliant type
> - Brand mission line (one sentence) at base
> - Brand wordmark + tagline at top-right
> - Free-from icons stack (no artificial colors, real fruit flavor, etc.) below the shields, smaller
>
> This is the *unbreakable rule.* Every SKU follows this. The system is the brand."

**MASSIMO:**
> "The grid is what makes this scalable. Same fixed positions for wordmark, shields, supplement facts across every SKU. Same fixed grid across Ice Pop, Fruit Snack, Chip, Chocolate Milk. Massimo unit-system discipline.
>
> Hand to Block 11 for the SKU-1 (Ice Pop) sketch. Hand to a future production-room session for the full system across all SKUs."
