# BLOCK 05.7: SECONDARY PLATFORM NOTES

> Per Davis directive: capture the platforms that *almost worked* but didn't lead — preserve the kernels for potential reuse without polluting the locked Crew + HQ direction.

---

## ③ MAKE A FACE — carried as content/social layer (not killed)

Davis read: *"More of a content-focused thing. Too hard for a launch core, but good as a layer."*

**Use:** apply the *Make a Face* lens to the Crew + HQ platform as the **social/content engine.**

- Every Crew member generates **face-content** (brain-freeze face, tongue-color reveal, surprise-flavor).
- Every Drop launches with a **face-challenge** (UGC hashtag, kid posts their face with the new flavor).
- TikTok / YouTube-shorts native — reaction-faces are the primary content unit kids consume.
- Pack-side: small face-photo or character-face on each Drop pack.

**Why it doesn't lead:** *Make a Face* alone is a content frame, not a brand world. The Crew + HQ gives it a world to live in. *Make a Face* gives the Crew + HQ a content engine.

---

## ④ TASTES LIKE TROUBLE — mischief-tone DNA carried, platform killed

Davis: *"Interesting — like Misfits of Energy — but doesn't communicate the better."*
Ishan: *"Doesn't communicate the upgrade. Burns parent-trust."*

**Use:** the *mischief-tone DNA* persists in the Crew's personality, NOT as the brand's positioning. Crew characters can be slightly mischievous (gets-into-the-lunchbox, shows-up-in-the-freezer-uninvited) without the brand BEING about trouble. Tone, not tagline.

**Why it doesn't lead:** *Tastes like trouble* signals "this is the snack that gets your kid in trouble" — parents read it as anti-restraint or sugar-overconsumption. The mischief is best at *character* layer, not *brand* layer.

---

## ② REAL ONES KNOW — killed (too sophisticated for the kid audience)

Davis: *"These kids can barely talk. They don't know 'real ones.' Hard for a new brand to start there."*

**Use:** none directly. The *insider-club / kid-cult* energy that motivated this platform partially lives in the Crew + HQ already (the kid feels in-on-it when they buy). But the verbal *"real ones know"* register is too old for the 5-9 year-old target.

**Future:** if the brand wants a *teen-adjacent* sub-line later (Wave 5+), the *Real Ones* register might be where 12+-year-olds want to encounter the brand.

---

## ⑤ THE GOOD STUFF — killed (overdone)

Davis: *"Good stuff has been overdone."*

**Use:** none. *Good stuff* as a category-claim is genuinely owned by too many other brands (Kraft, dozens of CPG). The brand should not stamp itself with the phrase.

**Caveat:** the *kid-vocab "good"* register may still appear in copy — *"these are good"* / *"the good kind"* — but never as the brand's central claim.

---

## ⑦ JUST GOOD — killed (Nike-coded)

Davis: *"This is like Nike — just do it. Too generic for us."*

**Use:** none. Generic-confident plays poorly without the 50 years of Nike brand-equity that makes *Just Do It* work.
