### Block 02 — Audit

**Davis pre-room directives (head of agency, before the room opened):**

> 1. *"We are starting with Otterpops but are going to expand it to other things for fruit snacks and other products."* → Platform-first, SKU-second. Wave 2/3 are *the* brand.
> 2. *"For what we want to be different can be how we insert into the ritual, it's more healthy, it can be a lot of things, but it HAS to innovate in some capacity — we are just figuring out what."* → Innovation mandate is mandatory; lane is open (then narrowed live, see below).
> 3. *"The brand needs to resonate beyond the category because we are expanding it — for example Kinder started as seasoning and now its going to marinades to sauces to everything else."* → Kinder-shape scalability. Name + voice + aesthetic must travel.

**Elliot live (during Block 02) — TRANSPARENCY as the mechanic:**

> *"Reverse mullet — Goodles also did a good job on it. Not being USDA-organic in your face. We're sticking to the middle. Kids lets you stick to the middle, you don't have to pick a point of view there. We're trying to stick to the middle of not being overly USDA, not being pure Otter Pops — that middle. And competing at pretty much identical price point.*
>
> *What matters more for this group is the **transparency.** Transparency in the pricing comparison, in the nutrition comparison, in the ingredients — all of it comes down to the **legibility of the transparency.** That has to be what they're going to do. Because in any of those regards, that's going to affect the buying decision. The turnoffs I saw in those personas were all 'I can make my own decision — empower me to do so.' Whether it's on the nutritional value comparison or the ingredient comparison — as long as you can empower them with that information, that's actually it."*

**Davis live (responding + narrowing innovation lane):**

> *"I agree. One thing — the friction-removal is great. Same shelf, same pricing, same routine. It's worth making explicit in Block 5 — because that's the everyday-foods-upgraded, no-behavior-change.*
>
> *And — needs to bring a functional benefit. So it's not so broad that this product couldn't have a functional benefit. That could be packaging. Actually — I think it has to be **nutrition.** You can't just sell high-fructose corn syrup in a better package. So the functional benefit is already captured in the value-per-nutrition."*

The deck is not committing to color direction or anything like that — that's just the default.*

### Block 03 — Insights

**C1 — Founding story (Ishan, verbatim de-rambled):**

> *"The founding story is the **pull from Kinder's and Walmart.** Joe — CEO of Kinder's — has a very strong relationship with Walmart. They've tagged him as their **disruptor of choice**. Walmart identified the gap: in non-Tier-1 stores (the mass-tier majority), the snack aisles don't provide functional benefit to children. Joe reached out to us to build the platform. The launch SKUs — Otter Pops, fruit snacks — that's Walmart's call: **they've got a fuck yes for Otter Pops and a fuck yes for fruit snacks.** All the work in the deck is what Elliot and I built on top of that — store walks, market analysis, Kinder's-team feedback. The more we've looked at it, the more we've been like — **'holy shit, this is a real fucking problem.'** And it's beyond just an Otter Pop, even though that might be where they want to start."*

→ **Demand-pull, not pain-point** origin story. Conviction was *earned through the work,* not inherited from biography. The founders ARE *the parents who noticed* (biographical reinforcement of Block 05 Big-Idea candidate).

**C1 — Product truth / what does the parent feel (Elliot + Ishan + Davis):**

> **Elliot:** *"I think we want the parent to feel **smart.** Walk away being like 'I made an easy choice. That was an easy win for me, the kid, and my wallet.'"*
>
> **Davis (less-load-bearing — dad-eye-roll flag):** *"The wife is shopping; the husband sees organic-coded pops and goes 'what the fuck are these for, why didn't you get Otter Pops.' Nice if the product is also something the parent eats."*
>
> **Elliot (building on Davis):** *"The parent wants to eat them too. Personally — I'd love an end-of-the-day snack that benefited my macros and micros that wasn't just straight sugar. I'm not gonna have an Otter Pop, but I would have one of these if it had real value."*
>
> **Ishan (raising the friction-elimination concern):** *"In my mind, you're **eliminating friction** — no fight with the kid, no fight with the dad. But eliminating friction is hard to sell as a brand — you're selling the absence of something, not the benefit of something. **What positive messaging do we build around 'everyday food upgraded' / friction-removal? I'd like the agency to generate this.**"* → Block 04 territory work.

**C2 — Consumer (the multi-touchpoint validation + Goodles refinement):**

> **Elliot:** *"Goodles is a **coastal brand** — launched in SF as a California brand, meant for coastal parents. We're talking about something for **mass retail value shoppers** — accessibility matters. They want excitement-to-reach for something new, but ALSO **don't want disappointment of regretting the choice.** Protecting against the downside — 'I'm not gonna lose out on having to throw this away.'"*
>
> **Ishan (the four-touchpoint validation framework — verbatim):** *"It has multiple time points. **Pick up the pack** — premium-quality, legit-feeling cues (Goodles uses 'established 1946'). **Price** — similar enough that I don't put it back. **Eat it** — 'that was actually really good.' **Check the label** — 'oh fuck, this is actually even better for me.' That's when we win them as a repeat customer. **Win-win-win-win, not disappointment after disappointment.**"*
>
> **Elliot (multi-mode interaction principle):** *"Goodles doesn't try to sell you immediately on it being healthy. **Three ways to interact with the product:** 'this looks good, I'll buy it' / 'this looks fun, I'll buy it' / 'this looks interesting, let me check what's in it.' Multiple ways to engage. The other Otter-Pop-tier brands throw everything at you at once → analysis paralysis → 'fuck it, I'm getting Otter Pops.'"*
>
> **Ishan (kid vocabulary + the legacy-brand naming lesson — verbatim):** *"The number one SKU at the freeze-pop shelf is called **Fun Pop.** Literally fucking called Fun Pop. Another good one — **Kool Pop** (with a K). Otter Pop is honestly the least clear at messaging to kids. Fruit snacks is fun, Gushers is fun, Fruit by the Foot is fun — the legacy brands did this really well, the better-for-you new entrants have lost it. **Fritos · Doritos · Cheetos · Oreo** — fun-sounding, single or double syllable, easy on a five-year-old tongue. JonnyPops doesn't have it. We could learn lessons from the 80s/90s branding people — it's fucking good."*
>
> **Davis (naming-is-more-important directive):** *"Log this for naming. Now that Ishan's saying this, **the naming is more important than I originally thought.** 4 All Snacks is good for parents but **too close to Honest Foods.** A little too coded. If we can find something more like Fun Pop — kid-forward — in the brand-name workshop, that helps a lot."*
>
> **Ishan (kid-influence research):** *"60%+ of this demographic has kids influencing the purchasing decision. We'd assumed kids were out of the loop with digital shopping — we were wrong. Kids hold inordinate influence — through YouTube, school, the nagging voice at home, the Instacart-elbow-grab."*

**C2 — Trial-risk for Maria + the two-value-narratives + Walmart upmarket migration (Davis + Elliot + Ishan):**

> **Davis (cost-if-I-get-it-wrong frame for Maria):** *"For the Maria demo — what's the cost if I get this wrong? She doesn't have the luxury of getting something her kids don't eat. It sits on the shelf."*
>
> **Elliot (consistency-seeking under trial-risk):** *"$20. I might be excited about a new brand and try it. But this customer is **looking for the consistency from what they know.** They'll put something back down if they think 'if the kids don't like it, this is gonna sit in my freezer, I'll have to throw it away.'"*
>
> **Davis (the emotional-hurt stack):** *"That's an **emotional hurt** — that money could have gone to another snack, vitamins, healthcare, anything. **Part of the gain is a potential loss.** That's why I like paying attention to **holistic nutritional value**, not just per-ounce price parity. When limited resources, *satiation* matters. Just saw something about **satiation index** — croissants vs chia seeds — makes so much sense."*
>
> **Davis (Walmart upmarket migration — hard discipline statement):** *"**Walmart is attracting wealthier consumers** — stated in their annual reports. Used-to-be-Whole-Foods shoppers now at Walmart because Whole Foods is too expensive. Even me — high-income bracket, can't afford Whole Foods. **Not all Walmart shoppers are our target.** We can't try to be everyone at the expense of being the brand we need to be. Most of our eight personas had low incomes — Walmart's consumer is shifting. Want that in the back of your mind."*
>
> **Ishan (research on $100k+ Walmart shoppers + content-strategy spine):** *"$100k+ shoppers are at Walmart because the item pairs with other stuff there, or Walmart stocked exactly what they'd researched online. For content strategy / marketing — **awareness and profile of functional benefit must be visible.** To Davis's earlier point about transparency-of-functional-benefit."*
>
> **Davis (personas as starting set):** *"These are **initial personas** — by the end we'll have tighter profiles. Might be these four adjusted, new ones, deleted ones. Want the agency to come out with **more suggestions** — who these people are, **drivers to purchase, barriers to purchase.**"*
>
> **Davis (two-value-narratives split — the central insight):** *"What's lost for a $100k shopper vs Maria is so different. **$100k+ shopper looking at macros = benefit, optimization. Maria = necessity, survival.** Maria sees vitamins added because **she can't afford the vitamins separately.** *'Do I have to buy vitamins AND the snack?'* — no. **You'd just buy the item.** One purchase replaces two."*

**C2 — The multi-channel platform mandate (Davis, verbatim):**

> *"Walmart is the demand pull — but for this to meet Joe's stated goal of a **$150-200M business**, this is beyond just Otter Pops and **beyond just Walmart.** This has to go **Walmart, Costco, Sam's Club, then grocery — Kroger, Albertsons, regional chains.** Whole Foods is a question mark.*
>
> *Joe has strong relationships at Costco too. **The Costco consumer is very different from Maria.** That's probably a gap in the personas — the initial eight were Walmart personas.*
>
> *Penetration math: **75% Walmart/Club, 25-40% Grocery, 25-40% C-store.** Don't over-index on the Walmart consumer."*

→ **Brand thesis upgraded to multi-channel + multi-category platform.** Same identity has to work at Walmart freezer door + Costco end-cap pallet + Kroger middle aisle + C-store impulse rack. Block 04 territories stress-tested against four shelf-contexts; Block 11 packs sketch a multi-format SKU system; Block 12 pre-launch runs three concurrent tracks (Walmart-shopper / Costco-shopper / Grocery+C-store).
→ **Persona gap acknowledged:** post-workshop persona-tightening deliverable now adds Costco/Sam's shopper + Kroger/Albertsons grocery shopper + C-store impulse shopper to the existing eight.
→ **Business math:** $150-200M target. Penetration discipline (Sharp) is now math-locked.
