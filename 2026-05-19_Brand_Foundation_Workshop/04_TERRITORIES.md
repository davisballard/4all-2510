## FOUNDER NOTES FOR THIS BLOCK (logged live in Block 03 C2, carried forward)

### The multi-channel platform mandate (Davis, verbatim)

> *"Walmart is the demand pull — but for this to meet Joe's stated goal of a **$150-200M business**, this is beyond just Otter Pops and **beyond just Walmart.** This has to go Walmart, Costco, Sam's Club, then grocery (Kroger, Albertsons, regional). Whole Foods is a question mark. The **Costco consumer is very different from Maria** — Joe's relationships at Costco are strong too. Penetration math: **75% Walmart/Club, 25-40% grocery, 25-40% c-store.** Don't over-index on the Walmart consumer."*

→ **Territory candidates in this block have to *travel across channels* — not just live on a Walmart freezer door.** A territory that wins at Walmart but doesn't make sense at a Costco end-cap is **disqualified.** A territory that wins at Costco but doesn't survive a c-store impulse-rack is **disqualified.** This is a *harder* discipline than the original Block 04 brief.

### Test grid — each territory candidate stress-tested across four shelf-contexts

| Shelf context | Test question |
|---|---|
| **Walmart freezer door** (12-inch tile, kid eye-level, parent-flip-discoverable) | Does the territory deliver the **four-touchpoint win-stack** at 12-inch tile? |
| **Costco end-cap pallet** (6-foot pallet, adult eye-level, seen from 15 feet) | Does the territory still read at *15-foot distance,* and does the *"Costco-curated trust"* read survive? |
| **Grocery middle aisle** (Kroger/Albertsons, eye-level mixed, brand-aware label-reader) | Does the territory land for a Kroger weekly-trip shopper who's between Walmart and Whole Foods? |
| **C-store impulse rack** (kid-with-allowance, one-second decision, $1-2 single SKU) | Can the territory survive a one-second-decision impulse moment? |

A territory clearing all four = anchor candidate. A territory clearing three of four = consider with caveats. A territory clearing fewer = burn.

### The Ishan-asked-for *positive-coded framing* requirement (carried from Block 03 C1)

Ishan flagged in Block 03 C1: *"Eliminating friction is the mechanism, but it's hard to sell — you're selling the absence of something. **I'd like the agency to generate positive-coded territory candidates we can react to.**"* This block delivers on that ask. **Friction-removal is the *mechanism;* every territory candidate names what the *positive thing the friction-removal delivers* is.** No territory ends in *"...so there's no friction"* — every territory ends in *"...so you get [positive thing]."*

### Two-value-narratives test (carried from Block 03 C2)

Each territory candidate has to support *both* reads simultaneously:
- **Maria read** — necessity, *"one purchase replaces two,"* survival math
- **$100k+ Costco/Walmart-migrant read** — benefit, optimization, *"macros at parity price"*

One territory, two consumer reads — without committing to either at the expense of the other.

## TERRITORY 1 — *"Quietly Made Better"* (Hegarty)

**HEGARTY:**
> "The most obvious territory hides in plain sight. It's in Ishan's deck mission. It's the bones of the insight. *Quietly Made Better* is a territory.
>
> What it means:
>
> - The brand doesn't *announce* itself as better. It just *is.* No screaming. No category-shaming.
> - The voice is **understated competent.** The Trader Joe's flyer voice. The Vacation voice. The Stripe voice. *We did the work; here's the product; you tell us.*
> - The aesthetic permission is bold colors, plain English, no marketing-performance.
> - The deprivation scene reads as: *all you wanted was the same thing without the catch.* We are the brand that just removed the catch.
>
> Brands that live in this territory: Stripe, Vacation sunscreen, Trader Joe's, Bobs Red Mill (in their good years), Patagonia in their early years before they got loud.
>
> The cultural punch: in a category — kids' snacks AND wellness — that's *loud about its goodness,* being *quiet* is the zag.
>
> Risks:
>
> - **Could read as boring.** *Quietly* could become *invisibly* on a shelf next to Otter Pops in neon orange.
> - **Could read as premium.** *Quiet* is a Stripe vibe and Stripe is premium-coded. We're explicitly NOT premium.
> - **May leave kids cold.** A kid doesn't choose 'quiet' off a freezer door.
>
> Mitigations:
>
> - The *loud* lives on the *front of pack* (kid territory). The *quiet* lives on the *back of pack* (parent territory). Reverse mullet again — the brand can be loud-color visually AND quiet-claim verbally. Two registers; one brand."

---

## TERRITORY 2 — *"The Parent Who Noticed"* (Julian + Steel)

**JULIAN:**
> "I want to give this brand a *POV* the founder can defend in interviews and the brand can hold for ten years. The POV: *we're for the parent who noticed.*
>
> What that means:
>
> - The brand assumes the parent already knows the system is failing their kid. We don't have to *explain* nutrition gaps. We don't have to *educate.* We *acknowledge* — *you noticed, we noticed, here's the thing.*
> - The voice is **conspiratorial — in the good way.** Not us-against-them; us-and-you-against-the-system. Liquid Death's voice has this quality; Aviation Gin's voice has this quality; Old Spice in its prime had this quality.
> - The aesthetic permission: loud, confident, anti-corporate. *We're not Big Food and we're not Whole Foods. We're the brand for the parent in between.*
> - The deprivation scene reads as: *finally, somebody noticed what I noticed.*"

**STEEL:**
> "The narrative gold here is the *act of noticing.* The deck's *'health foods are designed for the affluent buyer'* is a noticing. The *'same shelves from 1990'* is a noticing. The *'nutrition inadequacies start at 5 years old'* is a noticing.
>
> The brand is built by people who *noticed* the same things working families have been noticing — and decided to do something about it. That's the founder's literal story (Ishan + Elliot + Kinder's noticed the same gap), and it's also the consumer's story.
>
> POV-driven brands travel further than positioning-driven brands because they generate ongoing content from the POV itself. Liquid Death generates daily content from *'water marketing is preachy and weird and we will take it down.'* 4 All Snacks could generate daily content from *'the kids' snack category has been failing for 50 years and we noticed.'*"

**HEGARTY:**
> "I like this. Risks?"

**JULIAN:**
> "Two risks:
>
> 1. **Could tribal-code as anti-Big-Food OR anti-Whole-Foods.** If we lean too hard on noticing, we start naming names, and we lose the bipartisan position. Discipline required.
> 2. **Could come off as smug.** *We noticed and you didn't* is a bad register. The territory has to be *we noticed because you noticed,* not *we figured it out and now you can follow.*"

---

## TERRITORY 3 — *"The Catch"* / *"No Catch"* (Sutherland)

**SUTHERLAND:**
> "Counterintuitive territory candidate. Every other better-for-you brand says *we're better.* What if the brand instead built itself around the *category catch* — the moment of *what's the catch?*
>
> Every parent reaching for a healthier snack is unconsciously looking for the catch. *It's more expensive. It tastes worse. The kid will hate it. It has weird ingredients. It's only at Whole Foods. The package is silly.*
>
> The category has trained parents to *expect* a catch. The brand could be built around *we removed the catch.*
>
> Imagine the manifesto:
>
> > *We removed the price catch. Same shelf, same price.*
> > *We removed the taste catch. Tasty af.*
> > *We removed the routine catch. Same shopping trip, same freezer, same kid.*
> > *We removed the trust catch. Real ingredients you can read.*
> > *No catch.*
>
> The territory's tagline writes itself. *No catch.* Or *Better with no catch.*
>
> What I like about it:
>
> - It *acknowledges the consumer's defensive posture* — they've been burned by better-for-you brands before. We meet them where they are.
> - It's *humble* — we removed catches, we didn't invent the wheel.
> - It's *generative* — every claim, every ad, every social post can be framed as 'we removed the [X] catch.'
> - It's *defensible* — competitors can't show up and copy 'no catch' as easily as they could copy *'quietly made better'* (which any wellness brand could attempt to claim).
>
> What I'm less sure of:
>
> - It's *negative-framed* — defined by what we removed, not what we are. Could feel apologetic over time.
> - *'No catch'* as a tagline could read scammy in some contexts — sounds like an infomercial.
>
> But as a *territory* — a creative space, not a tagline lock — I think it's the strongest of the three, because it gives the brand a clear posture and an infinite content engine."

## ROOM DEBATE

**JULIAN:**
> "All three territories use the same foundational insight. The question is which *register* the brand lives in.
>
> - Territory 1 (Hegarty's *Quietly Made Better*) is a *register choice.*
> - Territory 2 (Steel + me, *The Parent Who Noticed*) is a *POV choice.*
> - Territory 3 (Sutherland's *No Catch*) is a *frame choice.*
>
> These aren't mutually exclusive. The brand could live in Territory 1's *register,* with Territory 2's *POV,* using Territory 3's *frame* as a recurring content engine. But for the Big Idea work in Block 05, we need to pick the one that *leads.*"

**HEGARTY:**
> "I push back on my own. Re-reading my territory, *Quietly Made Better* might be the *register* but not the *territory.* The territory has to be a *world* — and 'quiet' isn't a world, it's an adjective.
>
> If I rebuild Territory 1 as a world, it's something like — *The Brand That Just Works.* Where 'quietly' is the *how* and *just works* is the *what.* That's a Stripe-coded world, not a kids'-snack world. So maybe Territory 1 is the brand's *style* and one of the other two is the *world.*"

**STEEL:**
> "I'd argue Territory 2 (*The Parent Who Noticed*) is the strongest *world* because it has a *protagonist* — the parent. Worlds need protagonists. *Quietly Made Better* doesn't have a protagonist; the brand is the protagonist of its own restraint. *No Catch* has a frame but the protagonist is the catch, which is a thing-to-remove, not a person to anchor.
>
> 'The Parent Who Noticed' lets the brand cast the *parent* as the protagonist — and that's emotionally durable for ten years."

**SUTHERLAND:**
> "I think the right answer is *No Catch* as the *content frame* and *The Parent Who Noticed* as the *protagonist anchor.*
>
> Every piece of content is: *here's a thing the category does wrong, here's what we did instead* (No Catch frame). And every piece of content is *for* the parent who noticed (Parent Who Noticed protagonist).
>
> Big Idea sits at that intersection in Block 05."

**HEGARTY:**
> "Working theory then: converge to **Territory 2** (*The Parent Who Noticed*) as the dominant world, with **Territory 3** (*No Catch*) as the content engine inside it. Territory 1 (*Quietly Made Better*) becomes the *voice register* — McCabe pulls it forward in Block 08."

## STRESS-TEST AGAINST THE FOUR LAUNCH-TIER PERSONAS

**HEGARTY:**
> "Before we converge, stress-test the working theory against Maria, Donna, Garcias, Kim+Cal. Does *'The Parent Who Noticed'* + *'No Catch'* land for all four?"

### Maria
**STEEL:**
> "Maria *did* notice. She's the most class-aware noticer of the four. She's noticed that wellness costs more, that wellness brands aren't for her, that Big Food is what's affordable. She'd nod at *'for the parent who noticed.'* The *No Catch* frame especially lands — *'we removed the price catch'* hits Maria in the most material way. Yes."

### Donna
**STEEL:**
> "Donna's noticed something *different* — she's noticed wellness is preachy and coastal. *'For the parent who noticed'* fits her, but the noticing has to be framed plainly, not in coastal-NYT op-ed register. *No Catch* lands plainly — she'd understand the frame instantly. Yes, with discipline on register."

### Garcias

*[Hispanic-American Family-Shopper Voice pulled in]*

**HISPANIC-AMERICAN FAMILY-SHOPPER VOICE:**
> "The Garcias household — and especially the grandmother — has been noticing the category gap for two generations. They've been making the trade between *real food they trust* and *organic food the daughter-in-law wants.* *'For the parent who noticed'* lands as *'finalmente, alguien hizo algo.'* The *No Catch* frame lands as *price catch removed + cultural-tokenizing-catch removed + grandmother-distrust catch removed.* Yes."

### Kim + Cal
**FERRIER (pulled in from silent observer):**
> "Kim + Cal don't *notice* in the active sense — they're tired. But they *recognize* the pattern when it's named. *'For the parent who noticed'* is recognizable to them even if they wouldn't have phrased it themselves. The *No Catch* frame is *exactly* what they need — they have zero bandwidth for catches at 8pm Tuesday. Yes."

**HEGARTY:**
> "Four for four. Working theory holds."

**JULIAN:**
> "Run it against the deck's *We Are NOT.*
>
> - **NOT a wellness/health-foods brand.** *The Parent Who Noticed* doesn't perform wellness — it acknowledges the category's wellness failure. ✓
> - **NOT a premium brand.** *No Catch — same shelf, same price* is the literal opposite of premium framing. ✓
> - **NOT full of gross ingredients (some artificial OK for cost).** *No Catch* doesn't claim 100% clean — it claims *better at parity.* ✓
>
> Run it against *We Are.*
>
> - **Approachable & plainspoken.** *The Parent Who Noticed* is talking to the parent as an equal. ✓
> - **Bold, colorful, fun.** Loud color stays at packaging; the voice register is plain-confident. ✓
> - **Innovative.** *No Catch* lets us continually surface innovations (twist-off, vitamin mix, flavor lexicon update) as catches-removed. ✓
> - **'Natural glycemic' / 'clean sugar'.** Lives in the back-of-pack reverse-mullet shield work. ✓
>
> Clean across the board."
