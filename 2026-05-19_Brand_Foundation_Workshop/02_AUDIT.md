## DAVIS PRE-ROOM DIRECTIVES (HEAD OF AGENCY, BEFORE THE ROOM OPENED)

Three frames Davis fed in before Julian opened. The room holds these as load-bearing — every department audit reads against them, not around them.

1. **PLATFORM-FIRST, NOT SKU-FIRST.**
   > *"We are starting with Otterpops but are going to expand it to other things for fruit snacks and other products."*

   Otter Pop replacement is the **wedge,** not the brand. Wave 2 fruit snack, Wave 3 chip, and whatever comes after them are *the* brand. The deck has the platform vision (slide 9) but buries it under SKU-1 narrative. Julian leads the strategic-foundation read with platform thesis at the top, not at the bottom.

2. **INNOVATION MANDATE — MUST BE NUTRITION-DRIVEN.**
   > *Davis (live, narrowing):* "Needs to bring a functional benefit, so it's not so broad that this product couldn't have one. That could be packaging — actually no, I think it has to be **nutrition.** You can't just sell high-fructose corn syrup in a better package. The functional benefit is already captured in the *value-per-nutrition.*"

   The brand cannot rest on substitution-positioning ("quietly made better") OR on packaging-as-innovation. The load-bearing differentiation is **functional / nutritional delta at parity price.** Within nutrition, the *specific* innovation lane is still open — the two deck formulations (general health-boost: Ca/Mg/D/E/K2; energy & electrolyte: K/Mg/Ca/B-stack), the vitamin density vs. Otter Pops, the sugar profile, the fiber add. Block 05 picks the one we stake. **Packaging, ritual insertion, distribution, brand-as-platform** still matter as *amplifiers,* but the innovation *itself* lives in the formulation.

3. **TRANSPARENCY AS PLATFORM MECHANIC.**
   > *Elliot (live):* "Transparency. In the pricing comparison, the nutrition comparison, the ingredient comparison. The legibility of the transparency has to be the thing. The persona turnoffs were all *'I can make my own decision — empower me to do so.'* As long as you empower them with that information, that's actually it."

   This is the missing ingredient under the *kid-is-boss-of-kid* belief — the **parent-is-boss-of-parent** corollary. The brand doesn't tell the parent it's better. The brand **shows the comparison and lets the parent decide.** Price per unit vs. Otter Pops on the front. Nutrition delta vs. Otter Pops on the back. Ingredient list legible-first, brand-second. This is what *"reverse mullet"* actually means in voice terms — front for the kid, back for the parent who wants the receipts. Block 08 voice work inherits this; Block 11 packaging inherits this hard. **Closest tonal reference in the audit: Goodles** — middle-path, not organic-coded, not Kraft-coded, lets the parent read and decide.

4. **KINDER-SHAPE SCALABILITY.**
   > *"The brand needs to resonate beyond the category because we are expanding it — for example Kinder started as seasoning and now its going to marinades to sauces to everything else."*

   Kinder's own arc is the precedent: seasoning → marinades → sauces → everything. The name, the voice, the aesthetic all have to travel that same shape. If 4 All Snacks the brand can't move from frozen pop to fruit snack to chip to chocolate milk to whatever-Wave-4-is *without re-launching the brand,* we have the wrong identity. Block 06 names this as a hard gate on the brand-name decision. Block 07 inherits it as the test on aesthetic + character call.

5. **FRICTION-REMOVAL HAS TO BE EXPLICIT IN BLOCK 05.**
   > *Davis (live):* "Friction-removal is great. Same shelf, same pricing, same routine — it is worth making explicit in Block 5, because that's the everyday-foods-upgraded, no-behavior-change."

   Shotton already named this in his behavioral audit. The directive is to **lift it to the Big Idea altitude,** not leave it as a behavioral footnote. *"No behavior change required"* is itself a positioning. The brand asks the parent for **zero new effort** — and *delivers* the upgrade. That's the everyday-foods-upgraded thesis articulated as a system: same shelf, same price, same routine = no decision tax. Block 05 leads with this.

### 4. BEHAVIORAL LENS

**RICHARD SHOTTON:**
> "Strong behavioral foundation in the deck. Three things I'm marking:
>
> - **The cue × routine × reward loop** (Duhigg) is used correctly. *Rewards = hyper-palatable; routines = (re)writable; exposure × availability × palatability = preference formed.* The brand is positioned as a *substitution-into-routine* play, not a behavior-change play. That's the right move at this price tier — behavior change is expensive cognitive work and Maria + Donna don't have the bandwidth.
>
> - **The 'reverse mullet'** packaging principle is a *dual-system intervention.* System 1 (the kid's fast, intuitive, visual brain) sees the front of pack and reaches. System 2 (the parent's slower, reading brain) flips and reads the back. Two systems, one shelf moment, one product. That's behaviorally sound *and* operationally elegant.
>
> - **'It's not a partisan issue. Kids cross all aisles'** — bipartisan-by-design. Behaviorally this is right. Tribal coding (organic-coded vs. MAHA-coded) reduces in-group / out-group reach. The brand can't tribal-code. The four-parity model, when expressed visually, can't pattern-match to either lane.
>
> Where I want to push:
>
> - The deck is light on **social proof mechanics.** A new brand in a category with no incumbent better-for-you option needs visible social proof at scale to break through. UGC, parent-creator endorsements, in-store sampling — these are levers we work in Block 12 pre-launch.
> - **Friction-removal** is mentioned for Kim + Cal (*don't make me think*) but not generalized. Friction-removal is a Maria lever, a Pat lever, an Elliot-said-this-on-the-call lever. *'Same shelf, same price, same routine'* IS friction-removal positioning. Worth making it explicit in Block 05."

### 8. CPG / WALMART SHELF SPECIALIST (GUEST)

**CPG / WALMART SHELF SPECIALIST (composite voice):**
> "I've sat on the buyer side at Walmart for the freezer section. Let me tell you what I see when I read this deck.
>
> What's right:
>
> - **You understand the shelf is the brand.** Most CPG decks I see, the founder is talking about brand storytelling and content strategy. You're talking about the freezer door — that's the right altitude.
> - **You understand the buyer is the parent, not the kid, at Tier 1.** A lot of kid-snack brands lose sight of this and design for the kid alone. The reverse-mullet packaging principle solves it.
> - **You understand same-shelf-same-price is non-negotiable.** A lot of better-for-you brands try to upcharge 15% and tell the buyer 'we're worth it.' Walmart's freezer buyer doesn't care. Cost-per-unit math wins.
> - **The persona work is good.** Maria, Donna, Pat — these are real Walmart shoppers. Jess and Megan are real *Walmart+ pickup-order* shoppers. The deck doesn't conflate them.
>
> What's missing:
>
> - **Pack-format math.** You're going for Otter-Pops-parity. Otter Pops sells at Walmart in 12-count flexible tube boxes around $5-6, 80-count Costco mega-boxes around $6, and various sub-formats. *Which format is launch SKU? Which format is wave 2?* The deck doesn't say. The pack format gates everything — manufacturing cost, freezer real estate, the freezer-door aesthetic.
>   - ***Research answers part of this:*** *12-count tube is uncontested at Walmart freezer (tube pops live at 80-200ct, 12ct is the fruit-bar shape). $5.99 hits parity with Outshine + JonnyPops. The format gap is real and the launch SKU lands in it. See `07_Research/06_Walmart_Freezer_Aisle_Teardown.md`.*
> - **Slotting fees and trade promotional budget.** Walmart freezer slot at scale costs real money. End-cap during summer activation costs more. Demo / sampling costs more. *'Nationwide launch'* requires a trade-promotional commitment that should be a line item in the Kinder's pitch.
> - **Velocity assumptions.** What's your sell-through-per-store-per-week assumption? Walmart kills SKUs that don't move. A 4-store-week SKU is dead in 6 months. You need to know the velocity bar.
> - **Substantiation discipline.** *'With vitamins'* is OK. *'Boosts immunity'* is FDA structure/function disclaimer territory. *'Prevents nutritional gaps'* is health-claim territory and requires substantiation that won't survive scrutiny. Saul will gate this in production, but it's worth knowing now.
>
> What I'd flag for the founder pitch to Kinder's:
>
> - The four-parity model is *exactly* what a Walmart buyer wants to hear from a kid-snack brand. Most brands lead with health and lose on price. The price-parity-first framing is the unlock.
> - Bipartisan-by-design is *exactly* what Walmart wants to hear. Walmart serves red and blue America. A brand that tribal-codes either way costs them shelf flexibility.
> - The *'every grocery, every school lunch, every hospital meal, every Little League game'* platform vision lands at Walmart-merchandiser altitude. They like brands that can travel to multiple endcaps and contexts."
