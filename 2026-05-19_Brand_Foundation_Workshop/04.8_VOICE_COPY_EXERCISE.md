## MCCABE EXPLAINS HIS METHOD (per Davis's ask)

**McCabe:**
> *"Three rules. Tell the truth. Make it interesting. Make it memorable.*
>
> *Voice work doesn't start with adjectives like 'fun' or 'warm.' Those describe everybody's brand. Voice work starts with* ***truth*** *— what we actually know to be true about this product, this audience, this moment. Then you find the* ***attitude*** *— the posture from which we deliver that truth. Then you write the* ***rules*** *— what we always do, what we never do. Then you* ***write the lines*** *— for every surface the brand lives on. Then you* ***stress-test*** *— read each one out loud. Does it sound like* ***us***, *or does it sound like every other better-for-you kid-snack brand? Re-write what fails.*
>
> *Five sections. We work them in order. By the end, the brand has a voice that's specific, ownable, and useful across every comm. That's the work."*

# 1. THE TRUTH

**Three things we know to be true about this brand:**

1. **The product truth.** ***Snacks you used to grab. Now made for the kids of today.*** *(Locked canonical phrasing 2026-05-19, Davis.)* The kid-snack shelf has been stale since 1990 — kids who once reached for it have moved on. We rebuild it, same shelf, same price, made for the kids actually growing up now. **Second-person *"you"*** directly addresses the parent — their nostalgia, their recognition. **The parent's memory + the kid's present** in one sentence.
2. **The math truth.** *Five-plus snacks a day for twelve years compounds. Small upgrades repeated across a childhood add up to a meaningfully different kid.*
3. **The relationship truth.** *We make the snack a win for everyone — the kid eating it, the parent buying it, the family budget. Nobody concedes anything.*

Every line we write rolls up to one of these three truths. If a line doesn't ladder to any of them, it's not our line — it's somebody else's line we're borrowing.

---

# 2. THE ATTITUDE

Three adjectives. Not feelings. **Postures.**

- **WITH-YOU** *(not above-you, not below-you).* We talk like the friend in your circle who pays attention. We're standing next to you at the freezer door, not behind a podium.
- **PLAIN** *(not flowery, not academic, not preachy).* Five-letter words beat twelve-letter words. We don't say *holistic nutritional value* when we can say *better stuff.* We don't say *thoughtfully formulated* when we can say *made well.*
- **UNAPOLOGETIC** *(sure of ourselves, not embarrassed by what we're not).* We're not a wellness brand and we're not pretending to be. We use some sugar. We use small amounts of artificial. We're not all-organic. **We don't apologize for any of it.** And we make our own claims rather than chase someone else's. Joe's instinct: instead of *"All Natural,"* say ***"Nature Made"*** — a phrase that's true, owned, and doesn't beg permission from the regulatory or wellness register. That's the posture: **make the claims that are ours, don't chase claims that aren't.** *(Refined 2026-05-19 — was "Sure," sharpened to "Unapologetic" to capture the not-embarrassed-by-what-we're-not nuance.)*

---

# 3. THE RULES

**Always do:**

- ✓ **Convey the benefit, never the feature.** *(Elliot 2026-05-19, locked.)* Feature: *"100% Vitamin C."* Benefit: *"won't crash at 2pm."* Features are inventory; benefits are why anyone cares. The number-one rule. Like a clean investor deck — don't list everything you have, *say what the purpose is.* If a line is a feature-list, re-write to a benefit.
- ✓ Speak from our own block — *positive POV only* (Elliot's gate). Never *unlike X* / *wellness left* / *finally a brand that*.
- ✓ End at a win. Every comm closes on something earned, not something promised.
- ✓ Use kid-words when possible. *Snack* not *nutrition product.* *Win* not *advantage.* *Better* not *enhanced.*
- ✓ Short beats. Snap. *Small bites. Big win.* — that rhythm carries.
- ✓ Talk to the parent and the kid at the same time. Both have to feel addressed.

**Never do:**

- ✗ **Never stack features.** *"100% Vitamin C · Real Fruit · No Artificial Colors · 12 Vitamins"* is feature-stacking — it tells the parent *inventory,* not *what changes.* The brown-packaging trap. Burn.
- ✗ No *"kids"* spoken at a kid. Talking *to* kids about kids reads as adult-marketing.
- ✗ No fuel / energy / power claims we can't substantiate. We're a snack, not a supplement.
- ✗ No wellness-aisle vocabulary. *Wholesome · superfood · clean · organic · pure* are someone else's words.
- ✗ No condescension. *We know what's best for your kid* is what every wellness brand says. Burn that lane.
- ✗ No comparisons in the line itself — **including *implicit* ones.** *Same shelf. Same price.* standalone is OK because it's *our* fact, no second object. But *"same price as the snacks you were already buying"* / *"same shelf as the ones your kid already wants"* / *"ten percent better than X"* are all comparison-coded — the implicit second object burns the line. *Better* / *more* / *less than* are all comparison-trigger words; if you write one, ask *"better than what?"* — if the answer is *"someone else,"* re-write.

---

# 4. THE LINES — across every surface

The same truth lives in every comm but shapes to the medium. Each surface below: 3 example lines + a *what-makes-it-us* note.

---

### A. PACK FRONT *(3-7 words max — visible at shelf in 0.3 seconds)*

- ***Small bites. Big win.*** *(the tagline — primary)*
- ***Big Wins.*** *(brand mark dominant — alternate front)*
- ***Made for now.*** *(secondary message for variant packs — generational, positive-POV)*

*Why us:* Confident. Plain. Doesn't list claims. No features. No comparison (*"Better at every snack"* killed — *"better"* implies *"than what?"* — comparison-coded). Lets the visual carry the rest.

---

### B. PACK BACK *(short paragraph — the parent flips and reads)*

- ***Snacks you used to grab. Now made for the kids of today.*** *Same shelf. Same price. A little better at every snack — fewer crashes, more real fruit, more of the stuff that actually helps. Across the day, the week, the years they're growing in, the little betters add up.* ***Small bites. Big win.***

*Why us:* Opens on the locked canonical product-truth surface line. Second-person *"you"* talks straight to the parent — their nostalgia plus their kid's present in one beat. No feature-stack. No comparisons. *(Updated 2026-05-19 — canonical phrasing locked.)*

---

### C. SOCIAL CAPTION *(1-2 lines — TikTok/Instagram/YouTube-shorts native)*

- *Made for the lunchbox. Made for the freezer. Made for the kid you're still getting to shape.*
- *Built for the kid you're feeding. Stocked where you already shop.*
- *Small bites. Big win. Five times a day, every day, for twelve years. Math is on your side.*
- *Fewer crashes. More real fruit. A win every part of the day.*

*Why us:* Talks to the scrolling parent. Direct. Kid-recommendable to a friend. Has a rhythm that survives muted video. **All lines lead with benefit-coded language** — *"made for"* / *"built for"* / *"fewer crashes"* — not features. *(Updated 2026-05-19 — line #2's *"the aisle you were already going to"* killed as implicit-comparison-coded.)*

---

### D. EMAIL SUBJECT LINE *(4-6 words — open-rate gate)*

- *Small bites. Big win.*
- *The snack worth grabbing again.*
- *A win that's also a snack.*
- *Made for the kid still becoming one.*

*Why us:* Doesn't sound like a brand selling you something. Sounds like a friend dropping you a note. *(Updated 2026-05-19 — *"The snack you were buying anyway"* killed as implicit-comparison-coded.)*

---

### E. EMAIL OPENING *(2-3 sentences after the subject)*

- *Raising a kid is hard. Buying snacks shouldn't be. We're rebuilding the snacks kids used to grab — made for the kids actually growing up now. Same shelf. Same price. Across the day, the math adds up.*

*Why us:* Friendly opener. States the problem. States our move. Closes on the math. *(Updated 2026-05-19 — *"better"* killed as comparison-coded.)*

---

### F. OUT-OF-HOME / BILLBOARD *(3-5 words, BIG, readable at 60 mph)*

- ***SMALL BITES. BIG WIN.***
- ***RAISING A KID IS HARD. SNACKS SHOULDN'T BE.***
- ***WIN THE SNACK. WIN THE DAY.***

*Why us:* One message. Big rhythm. No body copy. The visual carries the rest.

---

### G. TRADE PITCH ONE-LINER *(to Joe + Walmart buyer)*

- *Big Wins is the first kid-snack brand built for the whole childhood — sold on the mass-tier shelf, at parity price, delivering real nutritional benefit at every snack, compounded across every part of the kid's day.*

*Why us:* Investable thesis stated in plain language. *"The first"* is defensible because nobody else has built around the whole-childhood frame. Benefits not features. No implicit comparisons. Lands in 22 seconds. *(Updated 2026-05-19 — *"ten percent better at every snack"* killed as comparison-coded; replaced with *"real nutritional benefit at every snack"* — benefit-coded, business-defensible without making a claim Saul gates.)*

---

### H. INFLUENCER BRIEF INTRO *(one line, partner-friendly)*

- *Big Wins is rebuilding the kid-snack aisle for the kids actually growing up now. Same shelf. Same price. A win every parent agrees on.*

*Why us:* No "please post about our product." Just a clean two-sentence statement a creator can repeat verbatim. *(Updated 2026-05-19 — *"snacks your kid already loves"* (wrong product truth) + *"just made better"* (comparison-coded) both killed.)*

---

### I. PR HEADLINE *(10 words max)*

- *Big Wins debuts: kid snacks built for the whole childhood.*
- *Same shelf, same price, whole-childhood thinking: Big Wins enters Walmart.*

*Why us:* Says the news. Says the angle. Survives a journalist's edit. *(Updated 2026-05-19 — *"better childhood"* killed as comparison-coded; replaced with *"whole-childhood thinking"* — benefit-coded.)*

---

### J. PRESS KIT ONE-LINER *(one sentence company description)*

- *Big Wins makes mass-market kid snacks designed to compound nutrition across a childhood — sold on the same mass-tier shelf families already shop, at parity price, with meaningful nutritional benefit at every snack.*

*Why us:* Defensible. Specific. Anti-wellness-fluff. Says what we do without making claims we can't prove. *(Updated 2026-05-19 — *"ten percent better at every snack"* + *"same price as the snacks families already buy"* both killed as comparison-coded; replaced with benefit + parity language that stands on its own.)*

---

# 5. THE STRESS TEST — what makes us *sound like us*

The test is simple: **if a competitor said the same thing, would it sound the same?** If yes, it's not our voice yet. Re-write.

| Generic-BFY-brand version | Our version |
|---|---|
| *"Nutritious snacks the whole family will love."* | *"Same shelf. Same price. A win every family agrees on."* |
| *"Made with wholesome ingredients."* | *"Made for the kid eating it."* |
| *"Empowering parents to make healthier choices."* | *"Raising a kid is hard enough without negotiating snacks."* |
| *"Boost your child's day with essential vitamins."* | *"Five small bites a day. Big win at the end of it."* |
| *"Real fruit. Real nutrition. Real fun."* | *"Snacks you used to grab. Now made for the kids of today."* |
| *"Snacks designed for growing minds."* | *"For the children whose entire childhood we still get to shape."* |

**The rule:** if you can swap our brand name for a competitor's brand name and the line still works → re-write. If only our brand name fits → ship it.

---

# 6. MANIFESTO OPENER *(one paragraph — the spine in voice)*

> *Raising a kid is hard enough without negotiating snacks.*
>
> *So we rebuilt the snacks. The ones you used to grab — now made for the kids of today. Same shelf. Same price. Same fun. Just a little better at every part of the day, every day, across the years they're still growing.*
>
> *Five small bites today. Five tomorrow. Across a childhood, that adds up. **Small bites. Big win.***
>
> *A win for the kid. A win for the parent. A win for the family.*

*Why us:* Opens on the parent's truth. Resolves it in plain talk. Lands the math. Closes on the win triplet. Every sentence ladders to one of the three truths. No feature-stacking. No comparison-coded language (*"the ones your kid already wants" / "the ones you were already paying for"* — both burned per Elliot's gate). Every word is a five-letter word or shorter except "negotiating," "childhood," and "rebuilt" — all intentional. *(Updated 2026-05-19.)*

---

# 7. WHAT'S LEFT FOR FOUNDERS TO REACT TO

This is the **starting voice playbook.** Founders mark:

1. **The attitude adjectives** — *With-You · Plain · Sure.* Replace any that doesn't ring true. Add one if there's a missing register.
2. **The rules** — kill any DO or DON'T that's wrong. Add any we're missing.
3. **The example lines** — pick the lines that feel most-us per surface. Strike the lines that don't.
4. **The manifesto opener** — does it sound like the brand? Or does it sound like a brand?

Once marked, this becomes the voice playbook every downstream copywriter (in-house or freelance) uses to write *anything* — pack, ad, email, social, PR, packaging variant, limited-edition drop. **Voice is the cheapest defensibility a brand has. We spend the next hour making ours specific.**
