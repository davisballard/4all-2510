# ★ PLATFORM ① — "THE CREW"

**Named by:** Hegarty (mascot-led, M&Ms-lineage)

### The thought

The brand is **a crew of recurring characters** — friendly, weird, distinct personalities — who **show up wherever good snacking happens.** Every SKU = one or more Crew members. Limited editions = guest Crew. Seasonal drops = Crew adventures. The Crew is the brand's face, voice, and world.

This is the **M&Ms / Cap'n Crunch / Pop-Tarts-characters lineage** — but designed for 2026 kids, not 1985 kids. **The Crew anchors the foundational quartet without saying any of it out loud:** wellness-left (Crew is here where wellness wasn't) · this-generation (Crew is *new* — never seen before, no parent-nostalgia baggage) · third-position (Crew isn't stale-incumbent or BFY-restrained — it's its own world) · kid's-day (Crew shows up across every part of the day = every SKU).

### The world

- The Crew lives in *the snack-world that exists between meals* — the after-school stretch, the lunchbox, the freezer at 4pm, the school-bus chatter.
- Slightly mischievous energy. NOT cartoonish-children-show-cute. More like *Sour Patch Kids characters* meets *Adventure Time creatures* meets *Pixar shorts* — a contemporary kid sensibility, not a 1990 one.
- The Crew has their own rules, hideouts, and inside jokes. Kids feel like they're *in on it* when they buy.

### The character(s)

**4-6 distinct Crew members** at launch. Each is:
- A creature with personality (not realistic — stylized, weird-cute, mascot-grade)
- Tied to a flavor/SKU (Strawberry kid is loud and dramatic, Grape kid is chill, Cherry kid is competitive, etc.)
- Speakable (each has a voice/catchphrase the kid can quote)
- Limited-edition-friendly (special Crew members for collabs, drops)

Crew species deliberately *not the otter* (Otter Pops has that). Not the tiger (Frosted Flakes). Could be:
- A frog-coded character (frogs are having a 2026 kid moment — calm but weird)
- A capybara (Gen Alpha-beloved animal, weird, friendly)
- A made-up creature (anti-anchoring on a real animal — invents a new species)
- Some kind of friendly weird-blobs (M&M-style but more distinct)

### Packaging implications (Block 11)

- **Front of pack:** the Crew member dominant. Real-food illustration secondary. Flavor name in kid-friendly type.
- **Back of pack:** a *Crew bio* — meet [character], what they like, what's in their tube. Continues the world.
- **Color system:** each Crew member has a signature color (still inside the saturated-loud-not-coastal-brown palette).
- **Limited editions:** new Crew member introduces a new flavor + new pack story.
- **Cross-SKU:** the Crew anchors freeze pop → fruit snack → chip → cereal. *Each SKU brings its own format but keeps the Crew alive.*

### Voice (Block 08)

- Crew speaks for itself — the brand's voice IS the Crew's voice.
- Kid-direct, slightly weird, plain English with personality (e.g., *"Hey. I'm Grape. I'm here. Eat me first."* / *"Strawberry. Loud. Don't be subtle about it."*)
- Manifesto-grade: the Crew's *origin story* + *house rules* + *what they stand for.*

### Flavor naming

Each flavor = a Crew member. Names are *kid-quotable* (single-word personality names: GRAPE · STRAWBERRY · CHERRY · TROPICAL — possibly with weird character names attached: e.g., GRAPE THE GREAT, STRAWBERRY SLIM).

### Ad-world

- Animated Crew adventures (kid-style, slightly weird, NOT preachy)
- Each ad = a Crew member dealing with something kid-relatable
- Social: each Crew member has its own TikTok / YouTube short presence
- Limited-edition drops trigger Crew "events"

### Social / content engine

- The Crew is **the most TikTok / Instagram / YouTube-shorts native Big Idea on this list.** Each Crew member can have its own micro-account with personality. Kids follow individual members. Building parasocial engagement at kid level = playground chatter is automatic.

### Sample tagline-tier surface lines (NOT the Big Idea — surfaces of it)

> ***"Snack with the Crew."***
> ***"Meet your snacks."***
> ***"The Crew's in the freezer."***

### What it forces

- **Heavy creative investment up front.** Designing 4-6 distinct memorable characters is real Fink + Giuseppe + Massimo work — not a sprint.
- **Character risk** — if the Crew designs miss, the whole platform suffers. High-floor / high-ceiling bet.
- **Anti-brand-flexibility.** Once the Crew is established, the brand can't easily pivot to a non-Crew identity without losing equity.
- **Long-payback.** Characters take 2-3 years to land cultural memory at scale (M&Ms, Frosted Flakes took longer). Worth it if the brand commits.

### Why this is the leading candidate

The Crew **carries the foundational quartet as DNA rather than as claim.** It's authentic to the product (snacks have personalities), it's deeply kid-coded, it scales infinitely across SKUs, it generates infinite content, it makes parents smile (mascot nostalgia is universal — *79% of US adults enjoy mascots*), it differentiates absolutely from the BFY-coordinated quadrant (no BFY brand has a Crew), and it makes the brand **playground-quotable from day 1** (kids ask for *"Grape"* by character name).

# ★ PLATFORM ② — "REAL ONES KNOW."

**Named by:** Sutherland (kid-cult / insider-coded)

### The thought

The brand is **for *"real ones"*** — kid-vocabulary for "people who know what's good, who are trusted, who get it." The brand operates as a **kid-cult / insider club.** Not stated overtly. Felt. Kids on the playground recognize the pack and trade head-nods. The brand wins by being *the one kids in the know reach for first.*

This is the **Doritos lineage** (bold/gamer/badass-cult-brand identity without ever stating it) — but kid-coded and authentic-not-aggressive.

### The world

- The world is *the kid-circle that recognizes good stuff.* Slightly insider. Slightly mysterious.
- The brand never claims authority or quality — *the kid does that for the brand* by being seen with it.
- Voice register: confident, casual, *kid-aspirational* (the kind of kid you want to be).
- No grown-up condescension. No "for kids" anywhere. The brand acts as if it's *for real ones,* full stop.

### The character / no character

**Optional mascot** — could work *with* a Crew or *without.* If no Crew, the brand is purely visual + verbal. If with Crew, the Crew members are themselves "real ones." Could lean toward NO mascot for this platform — the brand IS the cult, the kids are the characters.

### Packaging implications

- **Front:** confident, restrained-loud (saturated color + bold type + minimal claims). Looks like the kid who "knows" picked it.
- **Back:** insider language — *"You picked this. Real ones do."* Or a small ritual ("Bite the corner. Squeeze the middle. Slurp.")
- **Pack-codes:** small insider markers — *Real Ones #001 / #002 / #003* on each SKU like a numbered series. Makes it feel collectible.
- **Limited editions:** *Real Ones Drops* — numbered, dated, hyped scarcity. Kid-coded.

### Voice

- Confident, casual, never preachy
- Kid-aspirational: speaks as if the reader IS a real one already
- Manifesto: *"Real ones know what's good. Real ones eat what they like. Real ones don't ask for permission. Real ones know."*
- Tagline-tier surfaces: *"Real ones know."* / *"For the ones who know."*

### Flavor naming

- Simple, confident, owned: *Strawberry · Grape · Cherry · Tropical*
- OR: numbered/coded — *#1 Strawberry · #2 Grape* (the *Real Ones series*)
- Limited editions: *Real Ones Drop No. 7: Mango Chamoy.*

### Ad-world

- Kid POV. Hand-held. Real kids. Real moments. No condescension.
- *Real ones know* lines drop at the end like a quiet stamp.
- Aspirational without being adult-aspirational — kids see themselves as the *real ones.*

### Social / content engine

- "Real Ones" community-coded social. Followers feel like insiders.
- UGC-friendly: kids posting their "real ones" moments.
- Limited drops = hype-cycle content.

### Sample tagline-tier surface lines

> ***"Real ones know."***
> ***"For the ones who know."***

### What it forces

- **Confidence-on-credit.** The brand has to *act* like real ones already trust it, even at launch. If execution misses, the line reads as posturing.
- **Anti-mascot lean.** Pairing with The Crew dilutes both Big Ideas.
- **Voice-discipline-heavy.** Voice has to consistently feel insider without slipping into adult-jargon or actual exclusion. Hard to hold.
- **Kid-tribe targeting.** Some kids feel inside; some feel outside. The brand has to make the inside feel *attainable* (any kid can be a real one) without making it feel earned-by-purchase.

# ★ PLATFORM ③ — "MAKE A FACE."

**Named by:** Steel (kid-physical-truth)

### The thought

The brand **owns the kid-face during snacking** — the brain-freeze face, the tongue-color reveal, the surprised-by-flavor face, the *"oh-wait-this-is-actually-good"* face. The product literally makes you make a face. The brand makes the face the point.

**Authentic product truth** — every freeze pop makes a face. Every weird flavor makes a face. Every brain freeze makes a face. The brand is in the business of *manufacturing faces.*

### The world

- The brand world is *kid faces* in close-up — funny, surprised, satisfied, brain-frozen, tongue-stained.
- Slightly chaotic, very human, extremely shareable.
- Lives natively on social — faces are TikTok / Instagram / YouTube native content.

### The character / no character

**Optional mascot:** could have a recurring "face-maker" character — a creature that makes funny faces about every flavor. OR no mascot, faces ARE the brand. Probably stronger *without* a mascot — the kid-faces ARE the brand identity.

### Packaging implications

- **Front:** a face making a kid-relatable expression — could be illustrated (a kid-character making the face) or actual (photo-style kid face).
- **Back:** *"Made to make a face"* / brief instruction-like ("Squeeze. Slurp. Make your face.") / *the face you're going to make.*
- **Flavor naming:** each flavor named for *the face it makes.* THE BRAIN FREEZE one. THE TONGUE-PURPLE one. THE SURPRISE-CHERRY one.
- **Pack-as-content:** kids photograph their face with the pack, post it. Hashtag-native.

### Voice

- Plain English. Direct. Kid-shoulder-bump tone.
- *"Made to make a face."* / *"What face does it make?"* / *"Show us the face."*
- Manifesto: *"Some snacks just sit there. Ours make a face. The brain-freeze face. The tongue-purple face. The oh-wait-this-is-actually-good face. We make snacks that make faces. Pick a face. We'll bring the snack."*

### Flavor naming

- *The Brain Freeze. The Tongue Stain. The Surprise. The Squeeze. The Slurp.* (action-faces and outcome-faces)
- OR: kid-named flavors with face-context — *Brain-Freeze Cherry. Tongue-Purple Grape. Wide-Eye Tropical.*

### Ad-world

- Close-ups of kid faces in reaction shots. Almost dialogue-free.
- Faces. Faces. Faces. Each ad ends with the brand mark and the face-naming.
- Branded GIFs, reaction memes — face-content is *the brand's social asset.*

### Social / content engine

- **The MOST native to TikTok/YouTube-shorts of this list.** Reaction-faces ARE the content category kids consume. Brand = native content unit.
- Hashtags: #FaceCheck #BrainFreezeFace #TonguePurple
- UGC-engine: kids submit faces, brand reposts, kid gets fame.

### Sample tagline-tier surface lines

> ***"Made to make a face."***
> ***"What's your face?"***

### What it forces

- **Visual-dominant brand.** Word-light, face-heavy. McCabe's writing role shifts to *captioning faces.*
- **Face-photography-discipline.** Pack design + every visual = real faces or face-stylization. Authenticity gate.
- **Kid-trust-on-faces.** Faces are personal. Brand has to handle UGC carefully (consent, kid-safety on platforms).
- **Wave-3 chip / cereal:** does a chip make a face? *Maybe* (the crunch face, the spice face). Cereal? *The pour-and-stare face.* Scales but the freeze-pop is the strongest fit.

# ★ PLATFORM ④ — "TASTES LIKE TROUBLE."

**Named by:** Sutherland (mischief-attitude)

### The thought

The brand is **slight-mischief-energy.** Kid-tolerated-trouble. *A little bit wild, a little bit fun-trouble.* Kids love trouble (sour candies, Mountain Dew, Sour Patch Kids, the *forbidden* corner of the snack aisle). Parents tolerate snack-trouble (snack-trouble isn't *real* trouble). **The brand is the snack version of mischief.**

This is the **Sour Patch Kids / Mountain Dew / Pop Rocks lineage** — but kid-tolerable for the parent-buyer.

### The world

- Slightly mischievous, slightly anti-rules, ALL fun.
- The brand world is *what kids get away with* (in a wholesome way).
- Voice: cheeky, kid-vocab, never grown-up-mean.

### The character

**HIGH potential.** Could anchor a *trouble-maker* mascot — a small mischievous creature who *gets the kid into snack-trouble.* Like a friendly imp / weird trickster. Could be a recurring antagonist-of-the-parent figure (always sneaking a snack into the kid's lunchbox).

Alternative: a *crew* version with each crew member representing a different flavor-of-trouble.

### Packaging implications

- **Front:** bold, slightly weird, kid-magnet visual. Mascot doing something mildly-troubling (sneaking in, peeking out).
- **Color palette:** saturated, slightly off-kilter — not Otter Pops cleanliness, more Sour Patch Kids visual register.
- **Back:** *"Tastes like trouble"* + a kid-version of the *Sour Patch Kids* personality (a bit of irreverence).
- **Limited editions:** *"Extra Trouble"* drops with weirder flavors.

### Voice

- Cheeky. Kid-shoulder-bump. Slightly anti-mom-shame.
- Manifesto: *"Some snacks ask permission. Ours don't. They get into your kid's lunchbox. They turn the tongue colors. They make a mess. Tastes like trouble. Acts like a snack. Same shelf. Same price. Trouble included."*
- Tagline: *"Tastes like trouble."*

### Flavor naming

- *Trouble Cherry. Trouble Grape. Trouble Tropical.* (the brand IS the prefix)
- Limited editions: *Double Trouble (mango chamoy)*.

### Ad-world

- Mascot adventures: the trickster character causing snack-trouble (in a wholesome way).
- Kid-trouble vignettes: kid making a tongue-purple smile at the dinner table, kid hiding a freeze pop in the freezer for later.
- Social: prank-y / mischief-coded content.

### Social / content engine

- Strong on TikTok / YouTube where mischief content lives.
- UGC: kids posting their "trouble" moments (eating an extra one, getting a tongue color, etc.).
- Limited-edition drops = "trouble events."

### Sample tagline-tier surface lines

> ***"Tastes like trouble."***
> ***"A little trouble. A lot of yum."***

### What it forces

- **Parent-comfort-discipline.** *Trouble* has to be clearly wholesome-trouble, not anti-parent or anti-authority real-trouble. If execution feels mean-spirited, parents bounce.
- **Anti-virtuous register.** The brand can never perform restraint. *Trouble* is the consistent register.
- **Could conflict with FI-3 platform.** *Trouble* connotes single-act fun, not a whole-day system. Best for freeze pop + fruit snack; less natural for cereal.

# ◆ PLATFORM ⑤ — "THE GOOD STUFF."

**Named by:** McCabe (declarative-confident)

### The thought

The brand simply IS **the good stuff.** Not *healthy-good,* not *premium-good,* not *organic-good.* Just *good.* Kid-vocab definitional: *"Mom, can I have the good stuff?"* The brand owns *good* as a casual, unselfconscious quality-claim.

### The world

- Confident, plain-spoken, casual-authentic.
- The brand world is *the snack you say is good.*
- Less mascot-led, more brand-stamp-led.

### The character / mascot

**Optional.** Could be no mascot — just a strong wordmark + brand stamp. OR could have a tongue-in-cheek "Good Stuff" character (a quality-inspector creature who approves SKUs).

### Packaging implications

- **Front:** strong wordmark (the brand). The flavor. A "GOOD STUFF" stamp prominent.
- **Back:** *"The good stuff. Just what kids reach for."*
- **Color discipline:** saturated, confident, anti-cluttered.

### Voice

- Plain English. Confident. Definitional.
- *"It's the good stuff."* / *"The good stuff is back."* / *"More good stuff dropping soon."*
- Manifesto: *"There's the okay stuff. The fake stuff. The 'better-for-you' stuff that tastes like wood. And then there's the good stuff. Same shelf. Same price. The good stuff."*

### Flavor naming

- Simple, declarative: *Strawberry. Grape. Cherry.*
- Limited editions: *Good Stuff Limited: Mango Chamoy.*

### Ad-world

- Kid says it: *"This is the good stuff."*
- Parent says it back: *"Yeah, the good stuff."*
- Generative across kid + family situations.

### Sample tagline-tier surface lines

> ***"The good stuff."***
> ***"It's the good stuff."***

### What it forces

- **Confidence-on-credit risk.** Like Real Ones — the brand has to act like it's "the good stuff" at launch, before the credibility is earned.
- **Generic-risk.** *"Good stuff"* is unowned but also universal. Defensibility lives in aggressive consistent claim over years.
- **Less character-rich.** This platform leans verbal/stamp-led, not world-led. Smaller surface area than Crew or Trouble.

# ◆ PLATFORM ⑥ — "HEADQUARTERS."

**Named by:** Fink (visual world / brand-as-place)

### The thought

The brand is **the snack-headquarters / hideout** for kid-culture. A *place* (visual world) where good snacks live and good kid-moments happen. The brand isn't a tagline — it's *where you go* when snacking matters. Like a treehouse / clubhouse / hideout aesthetic, but contemporary kid-2026 (Roblox-aesthetic-influenced, NOT 1990 treehouse).

This is the **Lucky Charms / Disney-world / Pop-Tart-movie-world lineage** — brand-as-place.

### The world

- The brand has a *visible universe* that appears across packaging, ads, social, in-store.
- Could be a *Snack HQ / Snack Hideout / Snack Lab* — a contemporary kid-visual space where the brand "lives."
- Inhabitants of the HQ become characters (a friendly snack-engineer? A snack-detective? A snack-DJ?).

### The character(s)

**HIGH potential.** The HQ has a roster of inhabitants — could be a Crew (overlap with ①) or different specialists (the flavor-engineer, the snack-detective, the freezer-keeper). Each plays a role in the HQ's operations.

### Packaging implications

- **Front:** a kid-magnet view of the HQ + the SKU's role in the HQ ("From the Snack Lab" / "Direct from HQ").
- **Back:** glimpses into the HQ — different scenes per SKU. Each pack expands the world.
- **Color system:** the HQ has visual codes (signage, uniforms, color-coded zones) — brand identity scales from this.
- **Limited editions:** "From the Lab" / "HQ Drop #007" — collectible.

### Voice

- Plays in the HQ's voice — could be slightly Wes-Anderson-coded ("All operations conducted with the utmost gravitas. Snacks dispatched at peak frosty.")
- OR more kid-energetic: ("Welcome to HQ. We make the good stuff. You eat the good stuff. That's the deal.")
- Manifesto: an *HQ rules* / *manifesto-from-the-team-running-the-lab.*

### Flavor naming

- Each flavor = a "lab project" or "HQ release" — *Project Strawberry / HQ Drop: Grape / The Cherry Files.*
- Limited editions naturally fit (numbered drops).

### Ad-world

- Animated HQ adventures.
- Each ad = a different part of HQ operations (the freezer wing, the flavor lab, the lunchbox dispatch).
- Hugely rich content engine.

### Social / content engine

- HQ "operations" as TikTok / YouTube content (a day in the lab).
- Limited drops become "events" inside HQ.
- Kids can become "honorary HQ" via promotions.

### Sample tagline-tier surface lines

> ***"From HQ."***
> ***"Welcome to the snack HQ."***

### What it forces

- **Heaviest creative investment.** Building a world is bigger than building a Crew or a Stamp. Needs Fink + Giuseppe + serious visual development.
- **Long-build, high-ceiling.** Like Disney IP — payoff is enormous if done well, slow to materialize.
- **Anti-launch-speed.** Probably 6-9 months of design lead time to do well. Could be late for Walmart launch.

# ○ PLATFORM ⑦ — "JUST GOOD."

**Named by:** Steel (deliberate floor candidate)

### The thought

A safer, simpler version of ⑤. The brand is **just good.** No premium claim. No wellness claim. No mischief claim. Just *good.* Plain. Honest. Repeatable.

### The world

- Minimal-strong. Brand voice is calm-confident. No mascot, no world. Just a strong wordmark + saturated color + good product + consistent messaging.

### Voice

- Plainspoken, confident, anti-claim.
- *"Made well. That's it."* / *"Just good."*

### What it forces

- **Boring-risk.** No character, no world, no hook. Defensibility is *aggressive consistency over time.* Real risk: kids forget about it because there's nothing memorable.

### Sample tagline-tier surface lines

> ***"Just good."***
> ***"Made well. That's it."***

This is the *deliberate-safety-floor* candidate. The room argues: **don't pick this.** It's here as the *what-if-we-were-conservative* counterpoint. Every other candidate above is more interesting.

## WHAT MOVES BETWEEN PLATFORMS

A useful synthesis lens: **some of these could combine.**

- ① THE CREW + ⑥ HEADQUARTERS — the Crew lives in HQ. Strong combination.
- ① THE CREW + ④ TASTES LIKE TROUBLE — the Crew is a mischief crew. Stronger than either alone.
- ② REAL ONES + ⑤ GOOD STUFF — the insider knows what's "the good stuff." Verbal-led platform without mascot.
- ③ MAKE A FACE could layer ONTO almost any other platform as the brand's social-engine.

The founders may want to pick one *core* + one *layered* element.
