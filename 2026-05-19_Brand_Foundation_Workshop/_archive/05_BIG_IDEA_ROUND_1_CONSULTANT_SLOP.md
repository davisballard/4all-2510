# ★ TIER 1 — PUNCH HARD

The four candidates the room thinks land hardest. Each is named by a different agency voice; each represents a distinct posture for the brand.

---

## ① "WHAT KIDS RUN ON."

**Named by:** Hegarty (taking Davis's Snickers-analog directly)

### The thought

Four words. Kid-as-engine. Snack-as-fuel. The brand is **what fuels the kid through their day** — the same logic that made Snickers *"you're not yourself when you're hungry"* a 30-year line. We don't claim performance benefits we can't substantiate. We claim **role.** *We're what kids run on.*

The four foundational pieces all live inside this:
- **(FI-1 permission gap):** the parent has been buying things that *don't* fuel; we're the thing that does
- **(FI-2 cultural gap):** kid-of-this-generation runs on different stuff than kid-of-1990; we're built for who they are now
- **(FI-7 competitive gap):** the incumbents are stale fuel; the BFY upstarts pretend to be fuel but compromise; we ARE the fuel that's also fun
- **(FI-3 platform answer):** kids run on us all day — morning, lunch, after-school, after-dinner — the compounding IS the fuel

### Manifesto seed
> *"Kids run hard. They run loud. They run all day. This is what they run on. Real flavor. Real fuel. Made for the way they actually live. From morning to bedtime. From the freezer to the lunchbox to the after-school chase. The food kids run on. Built for kids. Eaten by everyone."*

### 30-second mental image
Quick cuts: kid sprinting through a backyard, kid eating a freeze pop on the back step, kid arguing about Roblox while inhaling a fruit snack, kid laughing too loud at a sleepover, kid drinking from a too-big cup at breakfast. Voice-over (parent, warm): *"Whatever they're running on — make it this."* End frame: the wordmark, the SKU, "what kids run on."

### Tagline candidate
> ***What kids run on.***

### Tradeoff it forces
Brand is *kid-first, parent-second.* The parent is in the role of *the one who provides the fuel,* not *the protagonist.* If the founders want the parent's emotional journey to lead the brand (the Block 02 "parent who noticed" frame), this isn't it. This brand is *about the kid.*

---

## ② "SNACKS THAT GREW UP."

**Named by:** Sutherland (counterintuitive)

### The thought

Three words. Plays on two meanings at once: **(a)** snacks themselves matured — they evolved past the 1970 formulation and 1990 packaging; **(b)** *we* grew up — the parents who ate Otter Pops as kids now know better, and the brand caught up with them.

The succession claim from FI-2 lives here without naming Otter Pops. The third-position from FI-7 lives here (we're not the kid-stale incumbent, not the BFY-arrested-development upstart — we're the snack that *actually matured*). The transition is implied — *something grew up.* Parents read it as their generation's-Otter-Pop coming-of-age. Kids read it as a permission to feel grown-up about their snack.

### Manifesto seed
> *"We grew up. Then we noticed our snacks hadn't. This is the snack we made when we paid attention. Same shelf. Same price. Real flavor. Real fuel. For every part of the day — morning, lunch, after school, after dinner. Snacks that grew up. With us. For them."*

### 30-second mental image
Split screen: 1985-vibe kid eating an Otter Pop. Adjacent: 2026 kid same age eating a 4 All Snacks freeze pop. The kids glance at each other across decades. Cut to the parent (former kid) watching the 2026 kid, smiling. Voice-over: *"Snacks that grew up. With us. For them."*

### Tagline candidate
> ***Snacks that grew up.***

### Tradeoff it forces
Brand is *generationally-coded* — leans into the *this generation's* claim. If the founders want to keep the brand *agelessly classic,* this is more specifically *generational.* It also implies *we improved on something* which is technically a comparison (though softer than *"unlike X"*) — Elliot's gate is borderline-respected, not fully clear.

---

## ③ "THIS IS WHAT KIDS EAT."

**Named by:** Steel (consumer truth)

### The thought

Five words. Declarative. Bold. **Bypasses the entire competitive conversation** by *stating the brand's role as fact.* Not *"we're the better choice"* (comparison). Not *"we're the next thing"* (succession). Just: **this is what kids eat now.**

Lives in the *Olly* logic — Olly didn't say *"better vitamins,"* they said *Sleep* / *Energy* / *Calm.* We don't say *"better snacks,"* we say *this is what kids eat.* The declaration creates the category. The brand defines the truth.

The platform compounding lives in *what kids eat* (across the whole day). The translation moat lives in the same posture (brand-as-category-truth). The succession lives in *now* (this is what they eat *now,* not what *we* ate).

### Manifesto seed
> *"This is what kids eat. Across the day. Across the week. Across the years they're growing in. Made for the kid eating it. Made for the day they're living. Same shelf. Same price. Real flavor. Real fuel. Made well, finally."*

### 30-second mental image
A kid pulls a freeze pop out of the freezer. Voice-over: *"This is what kids eat."* A kid grabs a fruit snack from a lunchbox. Same V/O. A kid reaches for a chip after school. *"This is what kids eat."* End frame, brand logo: *"This is what kids eat."* No claim. No comparison. Just declaration.

### Tagline candidate
> ***This is what kids eat.***

### Tradeoff it forces
The boldness requires confidence. Brand has to *earn the right* to make a declarative truth-claim about kids — packaging, voice, distribution all have to back it up. If the brand under-delivers, the line reads arrogant. **High-confidence brand or nothing.** Hegarty's read: *this is the line that becomes a 10-year asset if the brand has the goods.*

---

## ④ "BUILT FOR THE KID. BUILT FOR THE DAY."

**Named by:** McCabe (language)

### The thought

Two beats. Plain English. **Names the brand's commitment to both axes at once** — *kid* (kid-joy, kid-vocabulary, kid-fun, kid-respect) AND *day* (platform, compounding, every-part-of-the-day FI-3 logic). The repetition of *built for* signals craft + intention.

The four foundational pieces all live here:
- *Built for the kid* delivers FI-2 (this generation) + FI-5 (never compromise fun) + FI-4 (made with care)
- *Built for the day* delivers FI-3 (kid's-day platform) + the compounding-across-touchpoints logic
- *Built for* both = FI-7 (third position; we're built; we're not stale-incumbent or BFY-upstart)

Doubles as both Big Idea and tagline. Generative — every comm can riff: *"built for the lunchbox,"* *"built for the after-school chase,"* *"built for the first bite after dinner,"* *"built for the parent on the Instacart order."*

### Manifesto seed
> *"Built for the kid eating it. Built for the day they're living. From the morning rush to the after-dinner hour, the food kids reach for — made well, made fun, made for this. Same shelf. Same price. Built for them. Built for the day."*

### 30-second mental image
Hands-on-build sequence: parent prepping a lunchbox; kid pulling open a freezer; a fruit snack going from store to backpack to playground. Each scene paired with *"Built for the lunchbox" / "Built for the bus" / "Built for the chase."* Final: *"Built for the kid. Built for the day."* Wordmark.

### Tagline candidate
> ***Built for the kid. Built for the day.***

### Tradeoff it forces
The *built* framing pushes toward craft-emphasis brand (think: New Balance, Filson, Le Creuset). If the founders want a *playful-kid-magnet* brand voice, *built* might be too earnest. McCabe's defense: *built* reads earnest *and* warm — it's the same word a parent uses about their kid's bike or fort. It's intimate, not industrial.

# ◆ TIER 2 — STRONG SECOND

Four candidates with real teeth that didn't make the top four but have distinct postures the founders may want.

---

## ⑤ "SNACKS NAMED BY WHAT THEY DO."

**Named by:** McCabe (translation discipline as Big Idea)

### The thought

Five words. Olly's $300M move, made the brand's organizing thesis. **The brand's distinctive promise is that every SKU is named by its outcome,** not its ingredient. *Crash-Free* (after-dinner with magnesium). *Wake-Up* (breakfast with fiber + B12). *Through-The-Afternoon* (after-school with electrolytes). Etc.

This makes the **translation moat (FI-6) the actual Big Idea, not just a craft principle.** The brand wins by being the first to do for kid-snacks what Olly did for kid-vitamins. The competitive whitespace is named, owned, and defended through naming-as-positioning.

### Manifesto seed
> *"Other snacks tell you what's in them. We tell you what they do. Crash-Free. Wake-Up. Through-the-Afternoon. Lights-Out. Made for the kid eating them. Named for the kid eating them. Same shelf. Same price. Real flavor. Real outcomes."*

### 30-second mental image
Series of SKU shots, each with an outcome-name overlay: *Crash-Free / Wake-Up / Lights-Out / Through-the-Afternoon.* Brief title: *"Snacks named by what they do."* No further argument needed.

### Tagline candidate
> ***Snacks named by what they do.***

### Tradeoff it forces
Locks the brand into a specific SKU-naming convention — every SKU must be named by outcome. If a flavor lineup ever wants to be just *"Strawberry / Grape / Tropical"* it would violate the Big Idea. Strong brand-discipline ask. Saul gate: outcome names can't make medical claims (*"Crash-Free"* is borderline; *"Calmer Naps"* is over the line).

---

## ⑥ "MADE FOR THE KIDS EATING IT."

**Named by:** Steel (kid-respect as brand axis)

### The thought

Six words. Direct, grounded. **Flips the conversation away from the parent to the kid.** The brand is *for* the kid — the parent is in service of the kid eating it. This is the deck's *kid-is-boss-of-kid* belief made foundational.

Carries FI-2 (this generation) + FI-5 (kid-fun) + FI-4 (made with care) + implicitly FI-3 (the kid's day). The platform is built for the kid; the parent buys it because it's built well *for the kid.*

### Manifesto seed
> *"Made for the kids eating it. Made for the way they taste, the way they play, the way they reach. Made for the day they're living — morning, lunch, after school, after dinner. Same shelf. Same price. Made for the kids. Always."*

### 30-second mental image
Mostly kid POV — close-ups of kid hands grabbing a freeze pop, kid faces lighting up at first bite, kid mouths laughing while a flavor name hovers. Parent appears only at the very end — pulling the box off the shelf, satisfied half-smile. *"Made for the kids eating it."*

### Tagline candidate
> ***Made for the kids eating it.***

### Tradeoff it forces
*Made for kids* is a category cliché — every kid-snack brand technically is. The Big Idea succeeds only if the brand *means* it in a sharper way than competitors. Defensibility through *kid-respect-in-flavor-and-design,* not through claim. Requires the room to define *what "for the kid" actually means operationally* — Block 07 + Block 11 carry hard.

---

## ⑦ "SNACKS WITH SOMEWHERE TO BE."

**Named by:** Sutherland (counterintuitive — gives the snack agency)

### The thought

Five words. Anthropomorphizes the snack — gives it intent. *Somewhere to be* means: every SKU has a touchpoint in the kid's day where it earns its place. Breakfast SKU has somewhere to be (the morning). After-dinner SKU has somewhere to be (the wind-down). Every SKU is *expected* by the kid's day.

This is FI-3 (platform-as-kid's-day) compressed into an active framing — the snacks aren't just sitting on a shelf hoping; they *belong somewhere* in the kid's life. The brand is **anticipatory + intentional.**

### Manifesto seed
> *"Some snacks just sit there. Ours have somewhere to be. Morning. Lunchbox. After school. After dinner. Each one earned its place. Each one shows up when it's wanted. Built for the kid. Built for the day they're living. Same shelf. Same price."*

### 30-second mental image
Time-of-day cuts: morning kitchen counter, lunchbox, after-school freezer, after-dinner couch. Each scene has the matching SKU appearing exactly when the kid reaches for it. *"Snacks with somewhere to be."*

### Tagline candidate
> ***Snacks with somewhere to be.***

### Tradeoff it forces
The personification is clever but might be too cute for the brand's earnestness. Risk: reads as gimmicky. If founders want a serious-confidence brand, this leans too playful. If they want playful-warm, this lands.

---

## ⑧ "EVERY PART OF THE DAY. MADE WORTH IT."

**Named by:** Julian (platform + value-prop in one)

### The thought

Two beats. **Names FI-3 (platform) and FI-5 (never compromise) at once.** *Every part of the day* = the platform (breakfast → bedtime). *Made worth it* = each SKU earns its place by being genuinely worth eating (kid-fun AND parent-permission).

The compounding from FI-3 is implied — if every part of the day is *worth it,* the cumulative effect is meaningful.

### Manifesto seed
> *"Morning. Lunch. After school. After dinner. Every part of the day deserves a snack worth eating. Made for the kid. Made worth it. Same shelf. Same price. Real flavor. Real fuel. Every part. Every day."*

### 30-second mental image
Sequence of four day-moments — each with the matching SKU + kid reaction. Tag: *"Every part of the day. Made worth it."*

### Tagline candidate
> ***Made worth it. Every part of the day.***

### Tradeoff it forces
*Made worth it* is good but slightly soft compared to Tier 1 sharpness. Works hard as a *positioning* but might not be punchy enough as a *Big Idea.* Strongest if the founders want a calmer, more confident-restrained brand voice.

# ○ TIER 3 — SAFER BASELINE

Four candidates that are dependable, on-strategy, but less brave. Useful as floor — if Tier 1 + Tier 2 don't land, these are defendable territory.

---

## ⑨ "REAL FUEL. REAL FUN."

**Named by:** McCabe (writability)

Two beats. Captures FI-4 (substantive) + FI-5 (fun) in four words. Generic enough that competitors could conceivably claim it; specific enough to work as a tagline. Manifesto-ready. Plays directly with the Snickers-analog "fuel" framing without the boldness of *what kids run on.*

> Tagline: ***Real fuel. Real fun.***

---

## ⑩ "THE FOOD KIDS LOVE. MADE BETTER."

**Named by:** Julian (deck-compatible)

Six words. Closest to the founder-deck mission language ("everyday food upgraded"). Reads positively from our own block. Risk: feels like the deck's positioning translated to a tagline — i.e., the room didn't add much. Davis's main objection from earlier ("you're reformulating the shit we said") applies most strongly here.

> Tagline: ***The food kids love. Made better.***

---

## ⑪ "FOR THE KID GOING HARD. ALL DAY."

**Named by:** Hegarty (Snickers-adjacent + platform)

Seven words. Performance-positioned. *Going hard* is kid/young-parent vocabulary. *All day* delivers platform. Lands in similar space to *what kids run on* but more vernacular. Risk: *going hard* is slang-coded and may date faster than the brand needs.

> Tagline: ***For the kid going hard. All day.***

---

## ⑫ "SNACKS THAT EARN THEIR PLACE."

**Named by:** Julian (curator framing)

Five words. Brand-as-curator. Implies each SKU justifies its existence on the shelf, in the lunchbox, in the kid's hand. Connects to FI-3 (every touchpoint earns) + FI-4 (substantive). Risk: a little earnest, a little *food-marketing-coded.* But it carries depth.

> Tagline: ***Snacks that earn their place.***

## WHAT'S DELIBERATELY *NOT* IN THIS LIST

The archived candidates from the pre-workshop file (*For the parent who noticed* / *Same shelf. Same price. Quietly better.* / *Finally* / *Sameness as a feature* / etc.) were deliberately set aside. They lived in the *acknowledgment-and-removed-friction* register. The Tier-1 candidates above live in different registers:

- **① fuel-role** (Snickers-analog)
- **② succession** (generational)
- **③ declaration** (truth-claim)
- **④ craft** (built-for)

This is intentional. Davis's directive was to bring *new postures,* not refine the existing ones. The archived candidates may still resurface if the founders feel one of them is sharper than what's above — but the room argues that the foundational quartet + today's room-generated material *enables* sharper Big Ideas than the pre-workshop versions.
