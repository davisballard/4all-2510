## CANDIDATE 1 — *4All* (or *4All Snacks* / *4All Foods*)

### What's it doing?

A platform-name move. *4All* implies *for all* — inclusivity, accessibility, bipartisan-by-design. The numeric *4* is a phonetic shortcut common in mass-market kid brands (e.g., 4Kids, 4Pack). Three syllables compressed to one beat: *fer-all.*

### What McCabe sees

**MCCABE:**
> "Strengths:
>
> - **Plain.** *For all* is what the brand believes. The name *is* the worldview. Doesn't require explanation.
> - **Inclusive without performance.** Doesn't say *organic, all-natural, kid-safe* — it says *for all,* which is bigger.
> - **Walmart-shelf legibility:** *4All* reads in 1 second. The character mark could be the *4* alone as a brand icon — clean.
> - **Bipartisan-by-design:** *for all* is politically neutral. Reads to Maria, Donna, Garcias, Megan equally.
> - **Platform-extensible:** *4All Snacks* today, *4All Drinks* tomorrow, *4All Foods* as the holding entity. Architecture-ready.
>
> Weaknesses:
>
> - **Generic-risk.** Lots of brands use *4* as a shortcut. *4Kids,* *4Pack,* *4Life.* The mark needs distinctive treatment to not pattern-match to a wholesale lot.
> - **Could read corporate.** *4All Foods* sounds like a holding company, not a brand a kid would name out loud. *4All Snacks* is more consumer-facing.
> - **Could read political-by-accident.** *'For all'* has cultural overhangs in the US — *liberty and justice for all,* etc. Probably reads positively in most contexts; could read overly civic in others.
> - **Trademark risk.** *4 All Foods* and similar marks likely have prior registrations. Real legal lift to clear globally."

**HEGARTY:**
> "*4All* is the *platform-architecture* move. *Yes* is the *consumer-reaction* move. Different brands, both feasible."

## CANDIDATE 2 — *Natural Rascal*

### What's it doing?

A character-led naming move. *Natural* signals the formulation half (real-fruit-flavor, lower-glycemic, vitamins-in). *Rascal* signals the kid half — mischievous, playful, anti-perfect-child, anti-Disney. The two-word collision *Natural Rascal* is itself the brand's tension: the *natural* half pulls toward wellness, the *rascal* half pulls toward kid-as-agent. They cancel each other's worst tendencies.

### What McCabe sees

**MCCABE:**
> "Strengths:
>
> - **Distinctive.** *Natural Rascal* is unmistakable. No other brand owns that wordmark. Memorable.
> - **Built-in tension.** The two halves *fight* each other in a productive way. Wellness-coded *natural* + irreverent *rascal* = the brand's exact positioning compressed into two words.
> - **Character-extensible.** *Rascal* implies a character or characters. We could build a small cast of *Rascals* across SKUs.
> - **Bipartisan-by-design:** *rascal* is plain English. *Natural* is plain English. Neither tribal-codes.
> - **Voice-extensible:** the brand can speak *as* the rascal — kid-perspective copy, irreverent product-name lines.
>
> Weaknesses:
>
> - **The *Natural* half is dangerous.** The deck explicitly says we are NOT a wellness brand. *Natural Rascal* puts *natural* in the wordmark. Even if the back-of-pack tells the truth, the front-of-pack name signals *natural / wellness.* Maria might flinch; Donna definitely flinches.
> - **The *Rascal* half is dated.** Cool 1955, less cool 2026. Has a *Dennis the Menace* / *Little Rascals* register — nostalgia-coded for Gen X parents but could read out-of-touch to Millennial / Gen Z parents.
> - **Hispanic-American check:** *rascal* is a common-English word but doesn't have an obvious bilingual or Spanish equivalent. *Pícaro* would be the closest. Garcias-grandmother test: would she recognize and trust *Natural Rascal* as food? Probably not on first encounter.
> - **Mass-shelf legibility:** *Natural Rascal* is two long words at 6 syllables. Reads slower from 10 feet than *4All.*
> - **Trademark risk:** *Natural Rascal* may or may not clear, but it has a much smaller pool of prior art than *4All.*
>
> The killer issue: *Natural* in the wordmark."

**HEGARTY:**
> "*Natural Rascal* is the *thrilling* name. It's the name that could become beloved — the name parents remember and kids ask for. But it's carrying the wellness-coded weight of *natural,* and the deck explicitly says we're not that.
>
> Two options. Either we drop *Natural* (and we lose the wordmark tension), or we keep *Natural* and reconcile the contradiction in the brand voice."

**FINK:**
> "Wordmark-feasibility for *Natural Rascal* — strong character potential. The wordmark can carry illustration. A small mascot or character flourish around the *R* could give the brand visual personality fast.
>
> Wordmark-feasibility for *4All* — strong typographic potential. The *4* alone can be a brand icon. Stripped down to the *4* in a circle or a square block, the brand becomes design-system-ready immediately.
>
> These are two very different visual paths."

**MASSIMO:**
> "I'll bias slightly toward *4All* for typographic discipline. *4All* gives me a *system* — a numeral and a word that can be set in any weight, any color, any size, and read consistently. *Natural Rascal* is a *character* — it carries with it certain illustrative conventions that the brand will have to honor for ten years.
>
> System brands scale. Character brands fade in and out of cultural relevance.
>
> But *Natural Rascal* is more *memorable.* The trade-off is real."
