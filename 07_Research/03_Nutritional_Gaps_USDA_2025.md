# 4 All Snacks — Nutritional Gaps (USDA 2025)

**Source:** Ishan + Elliot strategy-session input deck, slides 32-34. Citing the *USDA Scientific Report of the 2025 Dietary Guidelines.*

This is the **substantiation layer** for the brand's "10% better nutrition" promise. It's also the **clinical truth** that lets a doctor, nurse, teacher say *"I support this product"* without it being a wellness claim. Block 03 (Insights) reads from this; Block 08 (Voice/Manifesto) cites it carefully (not as fear-bait — as the why-this-matters frame).

## HEI-2020 scores (overall dietary quality)

The **Healthy Eating Index 2020** measures dietary intake alignment with the *Dietary Guidelines for Americans.* Score is 0-100; 100 = full alignment.

> "Dietary intakes among children and adolescents ages 2 through 18 years fail to align with the Dietary Guidelines. The mean total and component HEI-2020 scores vary by age-sex group, with total HEI scores ranging from a low of 48 among males ages 14 through 18 years to a high of 59 among females ages 2 through 4 years.
>
> **Among children ages 5 through 8 years, the total HEI-2020 scores are 53 for males and 54 for females.** These scores are meaningfully lower than those for ages 2 through 4 years."

**Translation:** The drop-off at age 5 is *measured.* That's the deck's headline insight — *"Nutrition inadequacies start at 5 years old (first time in school without parental oversight) and compound for life."*

---

## Beverage-source energy share by age (sugar-sweetened beverages)

> "Sugar-sweetened beverages have significantly different contributions to daily energy intake from beverages between ages 2 through 5 years (18 percent), ages 6 through 11 years (37 percent), and ages 12 through 19 years (46 percent). Finally, 100% juice contributes 22 percent of energy intake from beverages among [children]."

**Translation:** **By ages 6-11, 37% of beverage calories are sugar-sweetened.** By 12-19, **46%.** This is the territory the freeze pop sits inside. The product isn't *adding* sugar to a kid's diet — it's *substituting into* the existing sugar load with vitamins / electrolytes / lower glycemic load attached.

## Underconsumed nutrients (5%+ below EAR or AI)

USDA flags these nutrients as undersupplied across age-sex groups (not "of public health concern," but below adequate intake):

- Dietary protein, vitamin A, thiamin (B1), riboflavin (B2), niacin (B3), B6, folate, B12, vitamin C, vitamin E, copper, iron, magnesium, phosphorus, zinc, iodine

## Overconsumed nutrients

- Children ages 1-3: copper, retinol, zinc, selenium (above UL)
- Males ages 4-8: zinc (above UL)

## How this maps to the Ice Pop "good in" formulations (slide 28)

The deck proposes **two functional Ice Pop variants:**

1. **General health boost:** Calcium · Magnesium · Vitamin D · Vitamin E · K2
2. **Energy & electrolyte balance:** Potassium · Magnesium · Calcium · Vitamin B1, B2, B3, B6, B12

The "general health boost" SKU **directly hits the most-undersupplied vitamins** for the 4-13 demo. Calcium (22-68% below EAR) + Vitamin D (92-96% below EAR) + Vitamin E (33-61% below EAR) are the three biggest gaps the deck calls out. That's substantiated, not aspirational.

The "energy / electrolyte" SKU hits the **sideline-snack use case** (sports / hot day / sweat replacement) with Potassium + Mg + B-complex — relevant after the 71% YouTube-kid-sports-content stat in the phygital brief.

## What's NOT in this data (acknowledged limitations)

- **No 4-8 male Vitamin D data is reliable** in the USDA sample (estimate marked `*`). Don't lead with that age-sex segment in claims; lead with 9-13 where the data is firm.
- **No micronutrient data on Hispanic-American children specifically** vs. white working-class vs. multi-racial. The "Multi-Gen Garcias" persona may have different nutritional profile than "30-Mile Donna" — but the deck doesn't have it. Don't claim something we can't substantiate.
- **No data on whether vitamin-fortified ice pops actually move HEI scores** in real-world consumption. The math is plausible (substituting vitamin-fortified for unfortified at parity calories = net micronutrient gain) but unmeasured. Don't promise outcomes; promise inputs.
