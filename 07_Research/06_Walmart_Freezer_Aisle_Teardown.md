## TL;DR — what Julian tells Davis in the first 30 seconds of Block 02

1. **The hero target ($5.99 / 12-count = $0.50/pop) is positioned in a NO MAN'S LAND that's actually the right place to be.** The bottom-shelf 80-count tube pops (Otter Pops, Pop-Ice, Fla-Vor-Ice) sit at **$5.88 for 80 pops = $0.07/pop.** The mid-tier 12-count fruit bars (Outshine, JonnyPops, Bomb Pop) sit at **$2.99–$6.49 for 12 = $0.25–$0.54/pop.** The "kid freeze pop at a real-pop price point" doesn't exist as a defined shelf position — every brand is either tube-pop cheap or fruit-bar premium. 4 All Snacks is proposing a **tube-pop format at fruit-bar price-per-pop** with a tube-pop usage occasion. This is genuinely uncontested shelf space.

2. **Otter Pops just lost its parent vote.** As of late 2024/2025 the brand introduced a **100% Fruit Juice, no-artificial-dye version** that prints beige (apple-juice concentrate is the carrier). Consumer reaction on Twitter and TikTok ran "polluted water / sewage water / disappointed" (Today.com, Bustle) and the brand kept the original dyed SKU on shelf alongside the reformulated one — meaning the category leader is literally running two products with split consumer love. **The premium-priced 80-count 100% Juice SKU sells at ~$15.04 vs. the dyed original at ~$5.88.** A 2.5x premium for "the better-for-you Otter Pop" already exists at shelf and parents are NOT loving it. This is the gap.

3. **The FDA dye phase-out (end of 2026) is a forced re-shelf event the whole category is bracing for.** RFK Jr.'s HHS directive (March 2025) asks producers to voluntarily phase out Red 40, Yellow 5, Yellow 6, Blue 1, Blue 2, Green 3 by end of 2026. Conagra, JM Smucker, General Mills, Kraft Heinz, Nestlé, Hershey all have public reformulation commitments. **Freezer-pop brands are conspicuously absent from those public commitments** — Otter Pops moved early, but Popsicle (Unilever), Pop-Ice, Fla-Vor-Ice (Jel Sert), Welch's licensed pops, Jolly Rancher pops all still carry artificial colors as of this teardown. A brand launching dye-free, NOT as a reformulation but as the founding formulation, lands in a category that's about to chaotically reformulate around it.

4. **Walmart's "Great Value" private label is THIN at the kid-tube-pop subcategory — and that's strategically important.** Walmart's Great Value freezer-pop SKUs visible online are a **12-count "Patriotic" / "Freedom" (red-white-blue) seasonal pack** at ~$1–$2 and a **20-count Junior Variety Pack** at ~$2. There is NO Great Value year-round 80-count tube-pop competing head-to-head with Otter Pops/Pop-Ice. Sam's Club fills that gap with Member's Mark Organic 52-count at $9.98 (the only mass-club organic tube pop). **The retailer-private-label hole at Walmart's tube-pop shelf is real** — and a national brand can fill it before Walmart's own buyer ships a Great Value Organic.

5. **The shelf is FORMAT-FRAGMENTED, not BRAND-fragmented.** Five distinct freezer-pop archetypes are competing for the same kid in the same trip: (a) plastic-tube freeze pops (Otter Pops/Pop-Ice/Fla-Vor-Ice/Welch's), (b) molded ice pops on a stick (Popsicle, Bomb Pop, Jolly Rancher), (c) fruit bars (Outshine, JonnyPops, Good & Gather, Whole Fruit), (d) electrolyte/hydration pops (Pedialyte, Pop Off, GoodPop Electrolyte), (e) premium oat-milk/dairy pops (Chloe's). Each archetype is in a different freezer door at a different shelf height at a different price. **4 All Snacks needs to pick a format-anchor and own one shelf position, not chase the whole aisle.** Tube-pop format = freezer door at endcap or bottom shelf = under-decorated visual real estate = the loud-wall opportunity is real.

## Adjacent freezer categories competing for the same dollar

The kid's freezer-aisle "snack" decision isn't actually freezer-pop-vs-freezer-pop. It's freezer-pop-vs-everything-else-in-the-freezer:

1. **Outshine fruit bars** (above) — strongest "healthy" cross-shop, sits in the SAME door as freezer pops in most Walmart layouts.
2. **Edy's / Dreyer's fruit bars** — sibling brand to Outshine; lower-tier price, similar format.
3. **Pedialyte Pops** (above) — pulls "hydration" dollar out of the snack budget.
4. **Klondike, Drumstick, Magnum mini-cones** — ice-cream-novelty crossover. 6-12 count, $4.99–$6.99.
5. **Ice cream sandwiches** (Great Value 12-count, Klondike, Skinny Cow) — the direct "kid frozen treat" alternative when pops feel insufficient.
6. **Pop-Tarts Frozen / Eggo bars** — frozen-aisle "kid breakfast" can spill into snack budget.
7. **Yasso Greek yogurt bars** — adult-coded but bought for kids in health-conscious households.

The relevant point for Block 04 white space: **the kid says "freezer treat," the parent decides the format.** A tube pop that contains the nutrition density of a fruit bar removes that internal-conflict from the cart trip.

**Verdict:** $0.50/pop at 12-count puts 4 All Snacks at **exact parity with Outshine and JonnyPops Classic** — the two strongest "better-for-you" parent-respected brands at Walmart-tier retail. This is the right number. It does NOT compete on price with Otter Pops/Pop-Ice (and shouldn't), AND it doesn't reach the JonnyPops 8ct premium ($0.811) where the parent gets sticker-shocked. **$0.50/pop is the price-point sweet spot for "the parent who noticed" — high enough to signal quality, low enough that the $5.99 ticket is impulse-friendly at checkout.**

The only watch-out: the **parent who's been buying Otter Pops at $0.07/pop now needs a story for why $0.50/pop is justified.** That's the brand's narrative job — "10% better nutrition + tasty af" needs to actually carry the 6.8x price gap from the legacy default. The math is brand work, not pricing work.

## Share-of-shelf observations

Synthesized from Walmart.com cat-page SERPs, Target.com (verifiable), retail trade press, and one Lemon8/TikTok in-store walkthroughs of summer freezer-aisle resets:

- **Eye-level (kid eye-level = 36–48" off floor) at Walmart freezer pop section** = **Popsicle (Unilever)** boxes, anchored by Tropicals + Scribblers + No-Sugar-Added. Unilever owns the most premium real estate.
- **Above eye-level (parent eye-level, 60–72")** = **Outshine fruit bars** + **JonnyPops** + Chloe's. Better-for-you brands sit higher = parent-skew, not kid-skew. This is intentional planogram logic.
- **Bottom shelf / floor stack** = **Otter Pops 80ct, Pop-Ice 80ct, Fla-Vor-Ice 200ct, Welch's 27ct giant boxes.** Mass-tube format is heavy and stocked in stack-out floor displays during summer.
- **Endcap (seasonal, Memorial Day → Labor Day)** = depending on store: Otter Pops endcap, Pop-Ice endcap, or Great Value seasonal "Patriotic" endcap. **The Great Value Patriotic 12ct is the SEASONAL endcap private-label play** — confirms Walmart treats 12-count tube as a seasonal-novelty format, not year-round.
- **Frozen-aisle door order (typical Walmart layout)** = ice cream sandwiches / cones → fruit bars → ice pops on a stick → mass tube pops at end of run. **Tube pops are the cheapest item in the freezer aisle.** A premium tube pop ($0.50/pop) breaks this hierarchy intentionally.
- **Visual brand identity** on the tube-pop shelf is overwhelmingly: clear plastic tube + 70s-cartoon-mascot + multi-color stripe pack (Otter Pops Louie-Bloo / Sir Isaac / Alexander the Grape character system; Pop-Ice rainbow bar; Fla-Vor-Ice Coca-Cola-red banner). **Reverse-mullet "loud wall" packaging would visually dominate this shelf** — every incumbent looks like a 1985 throwback.
- **In-store, Walmart tube pops are often shelved un-iced in the main grocery freezer section AND on dedicated room-temp summer displays** (because tubes are shelf-stable pre-freeze). This is unique to the format — molded pops can't do this. Endcap and door at the same time = double exposure.

## What's surprising / what's the gap

1. **Otter Pops has already broken trust with parents by reformulating.** This is the single biggest opening. The brand that owned "fun + indulgent + nostalgic" lost half its fan base by going beige and pricing the dye-free at 2.5x. 4 All Snacks isn't fighting incumbent love — it's fighting incumbent disappointment.

2. **The "fortified tube pop" (vitamins + electrolytes + low sugar in a freezer-pop tube format) doesn't really exist at Walmart.** Pop Off (online-only / niche) and GoodPop Electrolyte (limited Walmart distribution) are the only entries. Pedialyte owns the *medical* tube pop. **The everyday-snack tube pop with vitamins built in is a defined whitespace.** This maps directly to the 4 All Snacks Ice Pop innovation (good-in nutrition).

3. **No mainstream tube-pop brand has Hispanic-crossover flavors at Walmart shelf scale.** The category sits at the 1969 Big Six (strawberry/grape/lime/punch/berry/orange) plus minor tropical extensions. **Mango chamoy, watermelon agua fresca, spicy peach do not exist as mass-tube SKUs.** De La Calle owns Mexican-flavored *sodas* on shelf-stable; nobody owns it in freezer-pop. (Cross-ref: existing `04_Existing_Pop_Flavors_Audit.md`.)

4. **Walmart's Great Value tube-pop play is weak and seasonal.** No year-round 80-count Great Value tube pop competing with Otter Pops. This is the gap a national brand should close *before Walmart's PL team closes it themselves.* (Sam's Club has Member's Mark Organic 52ct — Walmart will eventually mirror this.)

5. **Popsicle (Unilever) is already running "Natural Colors from Fruit" on the 18-count Scribblers + Tropicals + Jolly Rancher.** The category leader on stick-format is already dye-free quietly. This is a *quiet shift* that hasn't been marketed loudly — meaning the "first to LOUDLY claim dye-free + better nutrition" position is still available even though product-level reformulation is underway.

6. **Sam's Club Member's Mark Organic Freezer Pops 52ct at $9.98 ($0.192/pop) is a 2023-launched private-label SKU that has been adding flavor variants quietly.** The fact that Walmart's sister club has run this for 2-3 years and Walmart-parent still hasn't extended Great Value to match is a signal: the buyer at Walmart proper has been WAITING for a national brand to validate the category before committing PL inventory. **4 All Snacks may be exactly what the Walmart frozen buyer is waiting for.**

7. **Chloe's Pops is in 10,000+ stores including Walmart but at a 24-pack premium format (2.5oz/pop, 60 cal) — it's a fruit-bar economic but a tube-aesthetic.** Underestimated competitor in the parent-conscious set, especially in the oat-milk / dairy-free positioning.

## How to use in workshop

### Block 02 — Audit (Julian's lead-in, 5-7 min)

**Opener:** *"The shelf you're going onto has five archetypes, not five competitors. The decision parents make at the Walmart freezer door is which FORMAT they trust — tube, stick, fruit bar, electrolyte, dairy. You're proposing tube — at fruit-bar economics. And the timing on this is incredible."*

**Three slides Julian pulls up:**

1. **The 5-archetype shelf** (Archetype 1-5 from this teardown) — visual grid of brands per archetype. Punchline: *"the shelf is not crowded, it's compartmentalized. You can own the loud-tube position with no one fighting you for it."*

2. **The Otter Pops reformulation case study** — side-by-side: dyed Original $5.88/80 (parent-dismissed) vs. beige 100% Juice $15.04/80 (parent-rejected). Today.com / Bustle quotes verbatim. Punchline: *"the category leader has already failed at the better-for-you transition. They left the door open. You're walking through it."*

3. **The price ladder** — $0.07 (mass tube) → $0.19 (Sam's Club organic / Otter Premium) → $0.33 (Popsicle / Outshine NSA Mini) → **$0.50 (Outshine / JonnyPops / 4 All target)** → $0.81 (JonnyPops premium 8ct). Punchline: *"$0.50/pop puts you at parity with the two most-respected parent brands at Walmart, in a tube format neither of them sells. You're both premium AND format-different. That's a rare position."*

### Block 04 — Territories (white space at shelf, 30 min)

Use the **"What's surprising / what's the gap"** section as the input list. Four territories the room can stress-test:

**Territory A: "The Loud Wall"** — visual whitespace. Every tube-pop incumbent is a 70s-cartoon-mascot pastel-stripe pack. Reverse-mullet loud-front + clean-back will dominate the shelf with zero ambiguity. (Cross-ref: existing `01_Category_Observations.md` De La Calle / Death reference.)

**Territory B: "The Better Tube"** — formulation whitespace. 10% better nutrition (less sugar, real fruit, no dyes, vitamins added) at fruit-bar economics in a tube format. **The parent who noticed Otter Pops went beige and was disappointed is now your customer.** Don't go beige — go vivid with natural color sources (beet, turmeric, spirulina, paprika — the playbook General Mills + Kraft Heinz are already running on other categories).

**Territory C: "The Flavor That Tastes Like 2026"** — the Big Six is 1969. Mango chamoy, watermelon agua fresca, spicy peach, strawberry passionfruit don't exist on any Walmart tube shelf. Hispanic crossover is the structural moat that Outshine + Popsicle + JonnyPops aren't built to chase (their flavor architecture is locked in legacy fruit). (Cross-ref: existing `04_Existing_Pop_Flavors_Audit.md`.)

**Territory D: "The Endcap Brand"** — the format whitespace. Tube pops are unique in being shelf-stable pre-freeze, which means **double exposure** — in-aisle freezer door AND room-temperature summer-display endcap. 4 All Snacks is the only entry that's deliberately *designing* for both placements with a loud-wall pack. Outshine can't endcap. JonnyPops can't endcap. Otter Pops endcaps but reads as cheap. **The premium endcap tube pop is uncontested.**

**The killer Block 04 sentence:** *"Walmart's frozen buyer has been watching Sam's Club run Member's Mark Organic 52-count for three years and hasn't extended Great Value. That means they're waiting for a national brand to prove the shopper before they commit PL. You're going onto that shelf at the exact moment the buyer wants you there."*
