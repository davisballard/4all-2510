## Why this is useful (and why it's not in the core files)

She's the **bottom-right matrix quadrant** — Megan/Jess-adjacent (app-first, multi-retailer, label-reading, granola-leaning). Outside the four launch-tier personas (Maria, Donna, Garcias, Kim+Cal) where the brand makes its money.

But she's *self-aware* about being outside the target — and her observations are useful in two ways:

1. **Validation from outside the target.** She independently surfaces several of the deck's diagnostic insights without prompting (the *"either Otter Pops or over-the-top granola but not granola"* false-binary, husband-as-secondary-consumer, sports-game distribution, Pedialyte-category-entry-point).
2. **Reaction-bar checks.** If a granola-leaning mom *outside* the target wouldn't bounce off the brand, that's a signal the brand isn't tribal-coding too far the other direction. The voice rules (bipartisan-by-design) hold even from this end of the spectrum.

She's *not* in the core files because:
- She's not Tier-1 launch audience.
- Her income + ingredient discipline are higher than the launch buyer's.
- Calibrating brand decisions to *her* purchase trigger over-rotates the brand toward Megan and away from Maria + Donna.

If the brand's voice or packaging accidentally lands well for her *and* the four launch personas, that's a free win. If it accidentally lands well for her *but not* the four, that's a course-correction signal — and the workshop should flag it.

## What she's actually saying (synthesis)

### Her purchase hierarchy

| Priority | Her position | Calibration to our brand |
|---|---|---|
| **#1 — No synthetic dyes** | Hard line. Red 5, Yellow 5 etc. Notes FDA phase-out coming via RFK | Confirms deck `We Are Not... full of gross ingredients (some artificial is OK for cost)` — but she draws the line at **synthetic dyes specifically.** This is a *common* hard line across granola-leaning moms. Worth pressure-testing the formulation. |
| **#2 — No high-fructose corn syrup** | Cane sugar OK, HFCS no | Per the deck — natural glycemic / clean sugar positioning aligns. Cane sugar is fine; HFCS is the bridge-too-far ingredient. |
| **#3 — Simple recognizable ingredients** | "Passable, recognizable" | Aligns with the back-of-pack reverse-mullet rule. Ingredient list should be readable, not a chemical lab. |
| **Tolerance for sugar** | Will allow sugar more readily than synthetic dye | Matches the deck's strategic move — keep sugar, fix the dyes / formulation. *Same shelf, same price, quietly better.* |
| **Behavior-change tolerance** | She has noticed her kids behave differently on sugar. Avoids it. | Sugar is not the brand's enemy — but the brand should be honest that this isn't a *no-sugar* product. Receipts on back, not promises on front. |

### Her secondary insights (worth holding)

1. **Husband-as-secondary-consumer.** *"If they're good enough and not cringe enough to be consumed by the husband. The husband's gonna keep them in the garage freezer. He's gonna go snag five of them on the way to the golf course."* — Independently surfaces what Davis raised on 5/12 (50% of Otter Pops eaten by parents) AND introduces a new persona vector: **the dad-secondary-consumer at the *garage freezer.*** The persona pack doesn't model this — Ron (Tween-Run dad) is episodic-buyer, not garage-freezer-thief. Worth flagging as a future-persona-expansion.

2. **Sporting events as the deflective trial moment.** *"Opening them up at a sporting event when they're not going to be judged for being too granola or too not granola."* — Confirms the sports-CEP + bipartisan-by-design hypothesis from the phygital sports brief and Block 12. The parent who hands out 4 All Snacks at the soccer game *doesn't get clowned* by other parents *either way.*

3. **Electrolytes as gimmick that works.** *"Electrolytes — it's one of those things where it probably doesn't do anything, but it's like a gimmick that might make people choose it."* — A granola-leaning mom's *pragmatic* read of functional claims. She knows the claim is light. She doesn't care. Functional claims *don't have to be material* — they have to *justify the purchase* without insulting the consumer's intelligence. This is exactly the *receipts, not promises* posture the voice rules already lock in.

4. **Pedialyte-as-category-entry-point insight.** *"Pedialyte pops are next to the regular electrolytes. People give them to their kids when they're sick."* — Davis already has this in `BRAND_MASTER.md` audience hypothesis section. The sister independently surfaces it as a *brilliant retail mechanic.* Worth flagging in Block 11 (packaging / pack-format) — is there a *sick-kid SKU variant* (the energy/electrolyte vitamin mix from deck slide 28) that could merchandise *off-shelf,* like Pedialyte, in the pharmacy aisle? That's a *Tier-3 + Tier-1 hybrid* play.

5. **Branding-bounce on wellness-coded competitors.** *"I won't get near that [a USDA-30% organic-slop competitor]. I hate the bottle. I hate the branding. It makes me feel like..."* — Even a *granola-leaning* mom rejects the over-organic-coded aesthetic. Confirms the deck's "Can't Blame 'Em For Not Wanting 'Em" insight from the OPPOSITE side of the binary — she's the audience the wellness brands *think* they're targeting and *she still bounces off them.* Strong validation of the loud-wall / non-organic-slop aesthetic direction in Block 07.
